# Lifetime Gross Profit (LGP)

*Customer revenue across their lifetime, minus the variable cost of delivering to them. The corrected version of LTV. The dollars that actually fund the next acquisition.*

---

## What it is

**Lifetime Gross Profit (LGP)** is the total gross profit a single customer produces over the lifetime of their relationship with the business. Sometimes written **LTGP**.

LGP starts with the revenue the customer pays you. Then it subtracts the variable cost of delivering whatever you sold them: the cost of goods, the labor hours that go into their account, the third-party fees that scale with them, the shipping. What is left is gross profit. Multiply by their expected lifetime (or, for subscription businesses, divide price by churn rate), and you have LGP.

## Why "LTV" alone misleads

Most operators say "LTV" (lifetime value) when they mean revenue. A $1,000-a-month customer who stays for five months is described as "a $5,000 LTV." That number is wrong, or at least worse than useless: it counts dollars the operator never gets to keep.

Alex Hormozi's correction is to insist on the **gross profit** version. The dollars that come back to you after the customer has been served. Those are the dollars that can fund acquisition, hiring, equipment, dividends, or growth. Revenue that gets consumed in delivery never reaches the reinvestment pool.

> "Most people say lifetime value and they think, okay, well, somebody pays me $1,000 a month for five months, that means their lifetime value is $5,000. No, that's not the lifetime value. You have to take out the cost of delivering on whatever service or good you have. And then you have the lifetime gross profit, because that is the profit that you'll be able to reinvest in growing the business."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, YouTube, 2024-06-30

## What stays in, what stays out

**In LGP** (subtract these from revenue):

- Cost of goods sold (COGS) for physical products
- Variable cost of delivery for services (the labor hours, tools, vendor fees, infrastructure that scale with the customer)
- Payment processing, shipping, and other per-transaction costs
- Anything that would not exist if that customer did not exist

**Out of LGP** (leave these in fixed costs, not subtracted from LGP):

- Rent, utilities, founder salaries, finance and legal headcount
- General marketing infrastructure
- Anything that exists whether or not a marginal customer signs up

The distinction is variable vs. fixed. LGP is about the marginal customer's contribution to the business, not about the business's bottom line.

## Why it is the load-bearing number

LGP is one of the three levers of [Customer Financed Acquisition](/principles/customer-financed-acquisition), the discipline of running the customer-acquisition loop so it pays for itself. The ratio of LGP to CAC tells you how many dollars come back per dollar of acquisition spend. A business that knows its honest LGP and improves it deliberately compounds. A business that confuses revenue for LGP eventually runs out of cash mid-flight.

The five operator levers for increasing LGP:

1. Raise price.
2. Decrease the variable cost of delivery (margins, automation, vendor renegotiation).
3. Increase purchase frequency (the customer buys more times).
4. Cross-sell (stack additional products onto each customer relationship).
5. Reduce churn (the customer stays longer).

The discipline is to monitor LGP monthly and run named initiatives against the levers above. Operators who can state the current LGP from memory tend to scale. Operators who cannot tend to stall.
