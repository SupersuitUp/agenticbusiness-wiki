# A-Player For You

*The A-player is not a generic archetype. They are the specific person whose strengths match your specific gaps. Write the job description from raw pain and raw aspiration, run it through AI, and the right person self-selects.*

---

*Distilled from [Sharran Srivatsaa](/mentors/sharran-srivatsaa), [public teaching short on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/0vE5SYe8HH8), 2026.*

## What it is

Every hire exists to do one of two things: relieve the pain of today, or unlock the growth of tomorrow. Most founders feel the pain or imagine the growth, then skip straight to a generic job description that hides both. The result is a posting that screens for B-players, who will accept any job, and that fails to register with A-players, who can tell the operator has not done the work to articulate what they actually need.

The principle: write the pain in full, ugly form. Or write the aspiration in full, specific form. Then run it through AI with the instruction to turn it into a job description. Post that. The A-player whose strengths match your situation will read it and recognize themselves in it.

> "Whenever you hire somebody, you're looking to solve the pain of today or the growth of tomorrow. The number one reason why it's difficult to find an A player is because we don't articulate exactly that."
>
> — Sharran Srivatsaa, 2026

## Why it matters

A-players read job descriptions carefully. The standard founder assumption is the opposite: that talent is busy, that they skim, that the JD is HR boilerplate the recruiter sends over. That assumption is wrong, and acting on it has a specific cost. The A-player who would have been your perfect hire reads the generic JD, concludes that you don't know what you actually need, and moves on. You are left with a stack of applicants who applied because they apply to everything.

The honest pain-and-aspiration JD reverses this. The right person reads it, feels the specificity, and recognizes that the seat was written for them. They do not need to be convinced. They self-select in.

> "The A player actually reads your job description. They actually care. So, if you think that your generic job description doesn't matter, you are wrong."
>
> — Sharran Srivatsaa, 2026

## How to do it

Two tracks, depending on what the hire is for. Use both if the role serves both.

**Pain track.** Write a raw document. Not polished, not professional. The actual list of what hurts. *I don't have time doing this. The quality is bad. The clients are having a worse experience than they should. Without this fixed, I am stressed and I am not sleeping. This specific workflow is taking three hours when it should take twenty minutes.* Upload that document to AI with the instruction:

> Turn all of this pain into a job description for the person who would relieve it.

**Aspiration track.** Write a different raw document. The list of things that would change the business if someone owned them. *Wouldn't it be amazing if we shipped a weekly product release without me touching it. Wouldn't it be amazing if every new client had a flawless first thirty days. Wouldn't it be amazing if we had a content engine that produced two long-form pieces a week.* Upload that document to AI with the instruction:

> Turn all of these aspirations into a job description for the person who would deliver them.

The AI translates the raw operator input into the document the candidate reads. The translation is the only thing the AI is doing. The substance comes from you.

## What good looks like

A finished pain-and-aspiration JD has four properties:

- **Specific.** The reader can tell exactly what is broken or what is being built. No generic phrases like "wear many hats" or "fast-paced environment".
- **Honest.** The pain is named. The JD does not pretend the business has no problems. Operators who are honest about pain attract operators who are honest about solutions.
- **High-aspiration.** Where the role is about growth, the aspiration is concrete enough that a person can picture the outcome. "Drive growth" is not an aspiration. "Take our onboarding from a three-week mess to a one-week white-glove experience for our top fifty customers" is an aspiration.
- **Self-filtering.** A reader who is wrong for the role can tell within thirty seconds. A reader who is right knows it immediately and feels addressed.

When the JD has these four properties, the inbound shifts. Fewer applicants, higher conviction per applicant, less back-and-forth in interviews.

## Common mistakes

- **Sanitizing the pain.** The pain document is supposed to be ugly. Editing it before AI sees it produces the generic JD you were trying to avoid.
- **Generic aspirations.** "Help us scale" is not an aspiration. The specifics are the magnet.
- **Using AI to polish instead of translate.** The AI is not there to make the writing fancy. It is there to convert a raw operator-side document into the candidate-facing one. If the output sounds like LinkedIn boilerplate, the input was thin.
- **Skipping the document, going straight to the prompt.** "Write me a job description for an operations lead" gives you the average job description for that title. Writing the pain and aspiration first is the entire principle. The prompt without the underlying document is theater.
- **Treating "A-player" as a portable label.** An A-player at a venture-backed seed startup may be a poor fit at a profitable family-office portfolio company. The phrase is meaningless without the context.

## The reframe

The hiring problem most operators describe as "I cannot find A-players" is, almost always, a different problem: they have not articulated the specific seat clearly enough for the right A-player to see themselves in it. Once articulated, the right person is not as rare as it felt.

> "You have now expressed exactly what you want, and they now feel like they are the perfect match for it. It's not that they are an A player. It is that they are an A player for you."
>
> — Sharran Srivatsaa, 2026
