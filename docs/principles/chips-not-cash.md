## What it is

The principle: when you have a comparative advantage (AI knowledge, applied AI implementation skill, domain expertise, distribution), do not trade it for cash. Trade it for equity in the businesses that are using your advantage. Cash is consumed in the trading. Equity compounds.

> "You've got a comparative advantage. And it's shrinking, just because the world is moving. So we almost gotta convert that AI knowledge into chips. Chips. Chips. You gotta have chips. Either poker or roulette or blackjack, however you want to use the word chips, but you're converting potential into financial assets."
>
> — Michael B. Ajouz, 2026-05-15

The word "chips" is intentional. They are stakes in the game. You play them, win or lose, but you own them. Cash for consulting hours is paid once and goes to expenses. Equity in a vehicle applying your AI knowledge keeps paying as the vehicle grows.

## Why it matters now

The clock is ticking. The same AI knowledge that is rare today will be common in 18 to 36 months. The operator who spent that window doing high-rate consulting will end the window with a bank balance. The operator who spent it converting know-how into equity will end it with positions in three to seven businesses that are operating at the leverage the knowledge created.

> "If your financials are cash, you'd lost. It needs to be equity. Or you don't compound. Equity, cash, know-how in a particular niche. That compounds. Right? Because the average AI intelligence will rise, you need to have specific AI."
>
> — Michael B. Ajouz, 2026-05-15

The structure of the deal matters. Not all equity is the same.

## How to structure the deal

Three properties of a chips-shaped deal.

**You are not just delivering the work.** A consulting engagement that converts to equity has to include real know-how transfer that lives in the operating model of the business after you leave. The equity is paying for the durable improvement, not the labor.

**You retain the relationship that makes the equity valuable.** A board seat, an ongoing advisory role, a contractual right to be consulted on major moves. Without ongoing connection, the equity becomes dead paper that gets diluted over time.

**The vehicle is in a niche where the comparative advantage compounds.** Equity in a generic startup competing in a crowded market is worth less than equity in a smaller business in a dominated niche. See [Niche Domination](/principles/niche-domination).

The wrong structure: a flat-rate consulting engagement that promises "future equity" or "potential equity" without the legal documents in place. That is cash work dressed in chip language.

## Worked example

The pattern Ajouz described for his own operating discipline:

He picks engagements where his strategic and operating knowledge can be applied to a small dominated niche. He participates in the equity of the resulting business. He stays connected to the business through board work or ongoing advisory. As the business compounds (often slowly at first, then faster as the niche position consolidates), his equity position appreciates. Across a portfolio of these positions, the math works out far better than a high-rate cash consulting practice would.

His specific case: hyper-niche insurance, where his comparative advantage (capital markets sophistication, operating discipline) is applied to an operator with insider community access. Both sides hold equity. Both sides care about the long-term result.

## What good looks like

After a year of chips-not-cash discipline:

- The operator has positions in three to seven businesses applying their advantage
- The positions are legally clean (documented equity, not handshake)
- The relationships are ongoing (board seats, contracts to consult, real participation)
- The mix of niches is intentional (not random or opportunistic)
- The operator can articulate why each position will compound, not just hope it will

If the operator finished the year with mostly cash and one or two scattered "potential equity" arrangements, the principle was not actually applied.

## Common mistakes

- **Cash now, equity later.** "Let me prove value first, then we'll talk about equity" is the most common trap. Once the cash is paid, the leverage to negotiate the equity is gone. Structure the equity up front or accept that this is a cash engagement.
- **Equity in the wrong vehicle.** Owning 2% of a generic startup chasing a crowded market is worth less than owning 5% of a smaller niche business with real cash flow. The compounding is downstream of the underlying vehicle's quality.
- **Equity without ongoing connection.** Becoming a passive shareholder dilutes the leverage you originally had. The chips have to stay live: ongoing relationship, ongoing influence, ongoing reason to make the position valuable.
- **Hoarding chips, never converting them.** Equity that never converts to liquidity is also a trap. At some point the operator has to take some chips off the table to fund the next round of conversion.
