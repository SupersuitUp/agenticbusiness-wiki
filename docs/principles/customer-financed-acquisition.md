## What it is

Customer-financed acquisition (CFA) is the operating discipline of running the customer-acquisition loop so the gross profit from existing customers funds the cost of acquiring the next ones. No credit line. No investor capital. The customer pays for the customer.

The discipline has three numbers in play:

1. **CAC**: cost to acquire a customer (ad spend + marketing payroll + sales payroll + commissions, divided by new customers in the period).
2. **[LGP](/concepts/lifetime-gross-profit)** (also written LTGP, lifetime gross profit): the dollars a customer pays you across their lifetime, minus the variable cost of delivering whatever you sold them. Fixed overhead stays out. Cost of delivery comes out.
3. **PPD**: payback period. The time from CAC spend to LGP exceeding CAC.

The ratio **LGP : CAC** tells you how many dollars come back per dollar spent. The **PPD** tells you how fast. Both numbers belong to the operator who wants the business to fund itself.

> "Most people say lifetime value and they think, okay, well, somebody pays me $1,000 a month for five months, that means their lifetime value is $5,000. No, that's not the lifetime value. You have to take out the cost of delivering on whatever service or good you have. And then you have the lifetime gross profit, because that is the profit that you'll be able to reinvest in growing the business."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, YouTube, 2024-06-30

## Why it matters

A high LGP:CAC ratio with a slow payback period is a business that needs financing. A modest ratio with a fast payback period is a business that prints. Both numbers matter and the second one is what most operators ignore.

Hormozi's mental picture: a dollar that returns $400 in ten years runs one cycle a decade. A dollar that returns $400 the same day runs three hundred and sixty-five cycles in a year. Same ratio. Wildly different businesses.

> "If I see a small business at a 100 to 1 LTV to CAC ratio and they have a reliable way of getting customers, then I know that this thing's gonna scale to the absolute moon."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, YouTube, 2024-06-30

A business that runs CFA tightly compounds in three ways at once. The ratio grows the gross profit per cycle. The payback period grows the number of cycles per year. The two compound multiplicatively. That is why some operators look like they are printing money and most are not.

## The working-capital adjacency: cash conversion cycle

In accounting, the **cash conversion cycle (CCC)** measures the number of days a business waits between paying for inventory and collecting from customers. The formula is CCC = DIO + DSO − DPO (days inventory + days receivables − days payables). The customer-acquisition analog is the payback period: same intuition, applied to CAC and LGP instead of inventory and receivables.

Some business structures run a **negative cash conversion cycle**, meaning the customer's cash arrives before the operator owes any of it back out. The classic mass-market case is Dell building computers only after the customer paid, then settling parts suppliers on net-60 terms. Amazon ran the same play at retail scale.

A coaching business that collects a $20,000 program fee at signing and pays subcontracted coaches on net-60 terms is running a negative cash conversion cycle. The customer is funding the operating model. Every new sale is a free working-capital injection. The structural advantage holds as long as the payment terms hold. The day that same business switches to monthly installments and pays vendors weekly, the advantage disappears and the business starts looking like every other service business.

CFA does not require negative CCC. But where the structure of the business permits it, it should be designed in deliberately. Annual paid-in-full plans, deposits, retainers, prepaid credit packs, and net-terms with vendors are the levers.

## Lever 1: Lower CAC

The acquisition funnel is a chain of percentages: an ad reaches an audience, a click rate filters down to a smaller group, a show rate filters further, a close rate produces customers. To lower CAC, improve the earliest, widest steps first. Small percentage improvements there compound across the largest audiences downstream.

The single biggest move in this lever is the **offer**: the thing you package together and put in front of the market. A better offer compresses CAC more than any single downstream conversion-rate optimization. The advertisement itself is the second biggest move, because click-through rate sets the size of every audience that follows.

Work CAC front-to-back. The smaller numbers are earlier, so percentage improvements have more leverage on the final cost per customer.

The clean outlier exception: if one downstream step is grossly below industry norm (a 5% show rate where 60% is standard), fix that first. But most of the time the biggest dollars are at the front of the funnel.

## Lever 2: Raise LGP

Five ways, in roughly decreasing ease of execution:

- **Raise the price.** The fastest lever. Most operators are mispriced low.
- **Decrease cost of delivery.** Better margins through automation, smaller delivery teams per customer, vendor renegotiation.
- **Increase purchase frequency.** Get the customer to buy more times across their lifetime.
- **Cross-sell.** Stack additional products onto the original purchase (the wallet that attaches to the iPhone case).
- **Reduce churn.** The customer stays longer, so the lifetime extends.

Build LGP back-to-front. Start with the most expensive, highest-margin thing you can sell a customer over their lifetime and engineer the catalog so a fraction of customers ascend to it. The back-end products inherit a CAC of zero (the customer was already acquired), so their full LGP stacks onto the original lifetime value.

**Stack-offers worked example.** A hair-extensions trainer with two products: a $2,000 extensions course (30:1 LGP:CAC) and a $15,000 business-coaching offer (7:1 LGP:CAC). The fix: stop advertising the coaching offer, lead exclusively with the extensions course, and ascend a fraction of buyers into coaching. If 20% ascend, blended LGP becomes $5,000 against the same $67 CAC, turning a 30:1 ratio into a 75:1 ratio. Same products, same customers, sharper sequencing.

## Lever 3: Shorten Payback Period

The lever most operators leave on the table. Three ways to compress PPD:

**Collect sooner.** Upfront payment, deposits, annual plans billed annually instead of monthly, paid pilots before paid implementations. Every day cut off the receivables side compounds across every cycle.

**Front-load the LGP.** Charge more for the first transaction. The customer who pays $5,000 at the door and $500 a month thereafter has a faster payback than the customer who pays $500 a month from day one, even if the lifetime LGP is identical.

**Compress time-to-value.** A customer who sees the value of the product in week one renews faster, refers faster, and ascends faster than a customer who has to wait six months for a result. Onboarding design is a payback-period lever, not just a customer-experience lever.

The compounding effect of this lever is what most operators miss. A business that cuts PPD from sixty days to thirty days has not just halved its working-capital requirement. It has doubled the number of acquisition cycles it can run per year on the same dollar of CAC. With LGP and CAC held constant, that change alone roughly doubles the rate of compounding.

## How the levers interact

The competitive moat shows up when the levers are tuned together.

> "If you have a massive LTV to CAC ratio, your CAC can double or triple and you're still printing money. And so that allows you to enter new marketing channels, new media channels, colder audiences, and outspend your competition."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, YouTube, 2024-06-30

A business with a 50:1 ratio and a thirty-day payback can outspend, outwait, and outscale a business with a 10:1 ratio and a one-year payback, even if both are honest businesses with real margin. Most acquisition channels are auctions for attention. The operator who can pay the most per click without going broke wins the channel.

The wealth-creation periods Hormozi names in his own career (Gym Launch year one at a 100:1 ratio, $100,000 of spend producing $10 million of return) were punctuated moments when all three levers happened to be aligned at once. The discipline is to engineer those conditions deliberately, then push as much volume through the loop as the operation can absorb.

## What good looks like

When CFA is being practiced:

- Every operator can state the current LGP:CAC ratio and the current PPD from memory.
- The numbers are computed honestly (LGP not revenue, full acquisition stack including payroll, PPD measured in days not vibes).
- All three levers are monitored and pulled as named initiatives, not background hope.
- New channels and campaigns are evaluated against the existing numbers before they get budget.
- Where the business model permits, payment terms are deliberately structured for negative or near-zero working capital.
- The team can name one specific move that would most improve each lever this quarter.

If the operator cannot state the three numbers in under thirty seconds, CFA is not being practiced yet.

## Common mistakes

- **Counting revenue as LGP.** Anything sold has a cost of delivery. Strip it out.
- **Loose CAC math.** Counting only ad spend and ignoring the people who run the ads, take the calls, and close the sales.
- **Optimizing only LGP:CAC and ignoring PPD.** A 50:1 ratio with a two-year payback is a worse business than a 10:1 ratio with a thirty-day payback. Cycle speed compounds.
- **Surrendering negative CCC for the wrong reason.** Switching prepaid customers to installment plans to "lower friction" without modeling the working-capital hit it creates. Sometimes the call is right. Make it consciously.
- **Optimizing late-funnel conversion before fixing the offer.** Biggest gains live at the front of the funnel where audiences are widest.
- **Scaling before knowing the numbers.** Most operators who stall under ten million per year are stuck here. Without the numbers, every additional dollar of spend is a guess.

## The operator discipline this principle implies

CFA is not a metric. It is a filter on what counts as business work.

> "If what you're doing is not getting you more customers cheaper, or making those customers worth more, then you are probably wasting your time."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, YouTube, 2024-06-30

Add the third lever to that filter. Every initiative, every hire, every product decision should move CAC, LGP, or PPD. If it does not move one of the three, it had better be solving for something else load-bearing (compliance, mission, safety). Otherwise, the work is decoration.
