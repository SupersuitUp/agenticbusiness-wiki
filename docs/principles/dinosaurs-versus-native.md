# Dinosaurs Versus Native

*Working with old industries requires commercial-sales skill and patience for slow-moving customers. Working in AI-native lanes moves fast but the basis of competition is intense. Pick which game you are playing, and play it on purpose.*

---

*Articulated by [Michael B. Ajouz](/mentors/mike-ajouz) in conversation, 2026-05-15.*

## What it is

Two distinct games are available to a founder right now. They look related but they operate by different physics. Mixing them is one of the most common ways founders waste years.

**The dinosaur game.** You take AI capability into pre-existing industries with established players, established workflows, established procurement processes. Banks, hospitals, schools, insurance carriers, government agencies, large enterprises. These industries move slowly. They have entrenched cultures. The skill required is commercial sales: relationship-building, RFP-navigating, year-long pilot cycles. The reward is large because the buyers have large budgets. The cost is your time.

**The AI-native game.** You build something that lives entirely in the new world. The customer is AI-native. The product runs on AI. You move at the speed of the model frontier, the speed of programming, the speed of distribution. No legacy buyers to convince. But the basis of competition is intense because every smart team is playing the same game.

> "If you want to do dinosaurs, that's gonna be one thing. That's like this. If you're gonna work with IBM, same thing. The second is, you know, you're living within AI, and everybody is new. So you don't do anything old, you're just so dominant here, and you're moving just at the speed of AI."
>
> — Michael B. Ajouz, 2026-05-15

The principle: pick. Build the business on purpose around one of these games. Hybrids tend to lose to operators who picked one.

## Why it matters

A founder running the dinosaur game with AI-native execution speed will frustrate the customer (who cannot move that fast) and burn the founder out (commercial sales cycles do not match the founder's energy if they are pulling from AI-native expectations).

A founder running the AI-native game with dinosaur instincts (long product-development cycles, custom-integration thinking, enterprise procurement instincts) will lose to the team that ships weekly.

Picking is the discipline. Once picked, run the game's playbook.

## How to choose

Three filters.

**Where is your comparative advantage?** If you have deep relationships in a specific old industry, the dinosaur game lets you cash in that advantage. If you are technical, fast, and unencumbered by institutional drag, the native game lets you compound on those attributes.

**What is your time horizon?** Dinosaur cycles are 12 to 36 months from first conversation to meaningful revenue. Native cycles are weeks. If your runway and your psychology cannot tolerate the dinosaur cycle, the choice is made.

**Do you actually want to deal with the buyer?** The dinosaur buyer is institutional. The native buyer is a builder, an operator, often technical. The personality fit matters. Founders who hate sitting in conference rooms with general counsels should not pick dinosaurs.

## Worked examples

From the conversation:

The IB-school applied AI pilot is a dinosaur game. The schools move slowly. The procurement is multi-stage. The decision involves boards and heads of school. The reward is real (every IB school in the country has the same problem) but the cycle is long. The strategy: bring AI capability to a small dominated niche of the old industry, get a credible champion, ride word-of-mouth.

Music industry deals are dinosaur-flavored: large labels, contractual complexity, relationship-driven. The workaround is to own a piece of the new layer (a video game, a media outlet, a community) that disintermediates the chokepoint while still operating in the legacy world.

A pure AI-native business example: building a developer tool for AI engineers. The buyer moves fast. The basis of competition is intense. The win comes from speed, taste, and distribution.

Most of Ajouz's own businesses sit in the dinosaur game because that is where his comparative advantage lives. His insurance business is squarely dinosaur. He explicitly recommends that builders with AI-native instincts spend more time in the native game.

> "If I was young, I would be totally happy dedicating my life here, moving fast, at the speed of the market. But what that means is you have to engage with your customer in a way that is native to that space. Whatever space, the basis of competition is just structurally different."
>
> — Michael B. Ajouz, 2026-05-15

## Mixed positions

There is a legitimate hybrid. A founder can take AI capability and apply it to a specific small niche of an old industry (the dinosaur game played at small scale), then use the equity from that to fund native-game positions. The hybrid works when the operator is honest that they are playing two games, structures the businesses separately, and resists the urge to use native-game expectations on the dinosaur business or vice versa.

Pure hybrid (a single business trying to be both at once) usually fails.

## What good looks like

A founder who has picked clearly:

- Can describe which game their business is in, and why they picked it
- Has structured the operating cadence to match the game (dinosaur: long calendar, relationship-heavy, patient; native: weekly ships, fast feedback, distribution-focused)
- Has not let the other game's rhythms infect their operating culture
- Knows what would tell them they picked wrong and is willing to switch if the signal appears

## Common mistakes

- **Picking dinosaur but operating native.** Founders frustrated that the bank is taking nine months to decide. The bank is not the problem. The game is. Speed up your expectations or pick a different game.
- **Picking native but operating dinosaur.** Founders building elaborate enterprise sales motions for products that should be self-serve. The product cycle gets compressed. The team gets miscalibrated.
- **Refusing to pick.** Trying to be both keeps the founder unfocused. The customer is confused. The team is confused. Pick.
- **Picking the game your network is in, not the game you would be best at.** Dinosaur founders with deep enterprise networks have a real advantage in the dinosaur game. Native-instinct founders forced into the dinosaur game because that is where their network sits will underperform. Sometimes the right move is to change networks, not to change the game.
