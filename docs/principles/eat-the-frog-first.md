# Eat the Frog First

## What it is

Brian Tracy popularized the image. If the first thing you did each morning was eat a live frog, nothing else that day could be worse, so you would move through the rest of it with the hardest thing already behind you. The frog is your hardest, highest-leverage task: the one you avoid, and the one that moves the business forward the most.

The discipline is to do that task first, before anything reactive. Before email, chat, the notification stack, the agent-review queue. One rule holds the whole thing together: you are not allowed to do anything else until the frog is eaten.

> "Success belongs to people who are willing to swallow the hardest bite before anything else."
>
> — Leila Hormozi, 2025-11-21

> "Do the hard thing first. Reward yourself second."
>
> — Leila Hormozi, 2025-11-21

## Why it matters

An agentic business runs an enormous reactive surface. Agents generate drafts, pipelines surface alerts, the inbox and the chat channels never empty. All of it is easy to touch, and all of it is happy to take your morning. The work that actually compounds is the opposite: the hard, ambiguous, judgment-heavy task that no agent can close for you. That task is the frog.

Decision quality is the scarce resource, and it is perishable. Hormozi tracks her own and refuses to spend it late.

> "I know I am not making decisions past 4 or 5 p.m."
>
> — Leila Hormozi, 2025-11-21

> "High performers tackle complexity when their mental resources are strongest."
>
> — Leila Hormozi, 2025-11-21

The operator who opens the laptop and starts clearing the reactive surface spends their sharpest hours on work an agent could have handled, then meets the frog at 4 p.m. with a depleted brain. The operator who eats the frog first spends peak cognition on the one task that needs it, then lets agents and easy execution fill the rest of the day.

## How to do it

- **Name tomorrow's frog tonight.** Pick the one task you have been avoiding because it is hard, the one that would move the business forward the most. Write it down before you sleep.
- **Do it before the swarm.** First block of the morning, ahead of email, chat, and every feed. Hormozi's rule: "I am not allowed to do anything else until it's done."
- **Spend peak cognition on judgment, not cleanup.** Route the reactive surface (agent-output review, inbox triage, status pings) to the back half of the day, after your decision quality has dropped. Defer any genuinely hard decision that lands late rather than making it tired.
- **Reward second.** A cleared inbox is the easiest first marshmallow in the building. Eat the frog, then take the reward.

## What good looks like

- The first ninety minutes of the day go to one hard, undelegatable task, every day.
- Email, chat, and agent-review queues stay closed until the frog is eaten.
- Hard decisions are scheduled into the morning, not absorbed whenever they happen to land.
- Late-day requests for a real judgment call get deferred to the next morning rather than answered at low resolution.
- The reactive surface still gets handled, in the back half of the day, where it belongs.

## Common mistakes

- **Opening the inbox first.** "Just a quick check" spends the sharpest hour of the day on other people's priorities. The frog is now an afternoon problem.
- **Mistaking busy for hard.** Clearing forty notifications feels productive and moves nothing. The frog is the task you are avoiding, not the pile you can grind through on autopilot.
- **Letting agents set the agenda.** An agentic stack produces an endless queue of things to review and approve. Reviewing is reactive work. It is not the frog, and it will eat the morning if you let it.
- **Making the hard call tired.** A judgment-heavy decision made at 5 p.m. is a worse decision. Move it to tomorrow's first block.
- **No frog named.** If you have not decided the night before what the frog is, you default to the easiest available task and call it work.

## The reframe

Most operators describe their problem as not having enough time for the important work. The time exists. It is being spent on the reactive surface during the exact hours when judgment is sharpest. Eat the frog first, and the important work gets your best cognition instead of your leftovers.

> "The things that you resist doing are always the things that will unlock your biggest growth."
>
> — Leila Hormozi, 2025-11-21
