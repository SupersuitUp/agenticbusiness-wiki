# Eight Questions, Two Pages

*A leadership team's entire vision and execution plan fits on two pages. Answer eight questions, agree as a team, and the document becomes the substrate every operating decision is checked against. If it doesn't fit on two pages, the vision isn't yet clear.*

*Distilled from [Gino Wickman](/mentors/gino-wickman), Traction: Get a Grip on Your Business, 2007. The tool is the Vision/Traction Organizer (V/TO), the two-page artifact that captures answers to the eight foundational questions.*

## What it is

Most companies write a vision document that is fifty pages long, that nobody reads, and that does not affect a single Monday-morning decision. EOS treats the vision as a *working document*. Two pages. Eight questions. The leadership team writes it together in a one- or two-day off-site, and they update it at every quarterly meeting.

The eight questions, verbatim from Gino:

1. What are your **core values**?
2. What is your **core focus**?
3. What is your **10-year target**?
4. What is your **marketing strategy**?
5. What is your **three-year picture**?
6. What is your **one-year plan**?
7. What are your **quarterly Rocks**?
8. What are your **issues**?

When all eight are answered, the leadership team agrees on every answer, and the document fits on two pages, you have a vision. Anything that does not pass those three tests is something else.

> "What is vision? It's clearly defining who and what your organization is, where it's going and how it's going to get there. It should be simple to articulate your vision, because it's probably already in your head. Unfortunately, if there are five people on your leadership team, there may be five different variations of the company vision. The goal is to get you all on the same page."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

## Why it matters

The vision document does one job: it makes every other operating decision faster. When a new opportunity comes up, the leadership team checks it against the core focus. When a [Rock](/principles/rocks-before-sand) candidate is proposed, it gets checked against the one-year plan. When a hire is debated, it gets checked against the core values.

When the vision is not written down, every decision is re-argued from first principles. Same conversations, every week, no resolution.

> "Most people are sitting on their own diamond mines. The surest ways to lose your diamond mine are to get bored, become overambitious, or start thinking that the grass is greener on the other side. Find your core focus, stick to it, and devote your time and resources to excelling at it."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

The two-page constraint is doing real work. It forces the team to compress, choose, and discard. A vision that needs fifty pages is a vision the team has not yet thought clearly enough about.

## The eight questions, briefly

Full treatment in *Traction*, chapters 3 to 4. The one-paragraph summary of each:

**1. Core values.** Three to seven timeless guiding principles that define the culture. Used to hire, fire, review, reward, and recognize. *Less is more.*

**2. Core focus.** The intersection of what the organization is genetically encoded to do and what its purpose is. Other operators have called this the mission, the vision, the niche, the hedgehog concept, or Unique Ability. Pick a name and stick to it.

**3. 10-year target.** A single specific, measurable, audacious goal a decade out. The whole team is unanimously motivated by it.

**4. Marketing strategy.** The target market, the three uniques that differentiate, the proven process, and the guarantee. One page.

**5. Three-year picture.** A painted picture of the organization three years from now. Measurables at the top (revenue, profit, target metric) and bullet points underneath. Not a list of obstacles, a picture of the destination.

**6. One-year plan.** Revenue and profit goals for the year, plus the three to seven most important goals to ship this year on the way to the three-year picture. Each goal is specific, measurable, and attainable.

**7. Quarterly Rocks.** Three to seven specific priorities for the next 90 days that put the team on track for the one-year plan. (See [Rocks Before Sand](/principles/rocks-before-sand).)

**8. Issues list.** The list of obstacles, opportunities, and ideas that the team has not yet addressed. Carried from quarter to quarter, worked down via [IDS](/principles/ids-the-issue).

The first four are the **Vision** half (questions 1 to 4). The last four are the **Traction** half (questions 5 to 8). The V in V/TO is the picture, the T in V/TO is how it gets executed.

## How to do it

**Run a one- or two-day off-site.** Leadership team only. No phones. The first attempt at the V/TO needs uninterrupted time. The questions are short, the conversation is not.

**Answer the questions in order.** Each one builds on the previous. Core values come before core focus, core focus before 10-year target, 10-year target before three-year picture, and so on. Do not skip ahead.

**Disagree out loud, agree before moving on.** A V/TO that has five different answers in five different heads is worth nothing. Stay on each question until the room genuinely agrees. This is where the work is.

**Write it on two pages.** Use the EOS V/TO template (freely available at eosworldwide.com/vto) or any clean two-page layout. The constraint is not stylistic, it is structural.

**Review every quarter, challenge every year.** At each quarterly meeting, walk the V/TO and update Rocks plus the Issues List. At the annual planning meeting, challenge everything: are the core values still right, is the core focus still right, is the 10-year target still motivating, is the marketing strategy still unique and valuable.

## What good looks like

A leadership team operating on a V/TO has the document open in the room at every quarterly meeting and at every Level 10 Meeting. Decisions get faster. New opportunities are tested against the core focus in thirty seconds. Disagreements about direction stop happening because the document already answered the disagreement.

New leadership-team hires can be onboarded on the V/TO in an hour. The document is the team's shared brain.

## Common mistakes

- **Writing a vision longer than two pages.** The constraint is the discipline. If the vision needs five pages, the team has not finished thinking yet.
- **Letting one person write it.** The V/TO is a team artifact. A V/TO written by the founder alone gets ratified, never lived. Write it together.
- **Skipping the core values exercise.** It feels soft and slow. Companies that skip it end up making every people decision (hire, fire, promote) without a shared compass, which is far more expensive over the long run.
- **Confusing the core focus with the current product line.** Core focus is what the organization is uniquely good at, not what it happens to sell today. A clear core focus often kills a product line and accelerates growth as a result (Gino's Image One example).
- **Not revisiting the V/TO every quarter.** A V/TO that lives in a binder is a paperweight. It earns its keep only when it is open every quarter and walked every Level 10.

## The reframe

Most teams treat "vision" as a one-time wordsmithing exercise, then file it away. The V/TO is the opposite: it is the leadership team's operating document, the substrate every other decision sits on. Two pages, eight questions, walked every quarter. When it works, almost every recurring strategic argument the team used to have stops happening, because the document already settled it.
