# Five Bullets Per Seat

*Every seat in the org is defined by no more than five outcome-bullets, what the person is responsible for. The tasks they use to deliver those outcomes are theirs to figure out. Compression is the price of accountability.*

## What it is

The accountability chart is the foundation of organizational health. Three steps: define the seats (functions) of the business, define the roles in each seat, put the right name in each seat. The discipline that breaks down for most operators is step two.

When asked to define a seat, most founders list everything that has to get done. The marketing seat ends up with twenty bullets, logos, website, PR, photography, lead nurturing, advertising, social, content, events, brand guidelines, agency management, copy, video, analytics, email, SEO, paid search. Every one of those is real. Together they describe the workload. They do not describe the seat.

The principle is the compression: no more than five bullets per seat, where each bullet is an outcome the person owns. What they are responsible for, not a task they perform. The how is theirs.

> "What is marketing's role is a very different question than what is marketing responsible for."
>
> Rick Fawcett, [Strengthening the Accountability Chart](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=T_ob72LrCp0), 2021

## Why it matters

Three failure modes show up when a seat has twenty bullets instead of five.

**The seat-holder stops owning the outcome.** They know they have to do the lead nurturing program. They do not realize their job is to create leads. The list of tasks crowds out the outcome. The person becomes a list-executor rather than an outcome-owner, and the moment the list does not cover a new situation, no one knows what to do.

**The scorecard explodes.** If every bullet needs a metric, a seat with seventeen bullets needs seventeen metrics. Five reports to the same manager means an eighty-five-metric scorecard. Nobody can see the business through that. The numbers stop driving decisions because there are too many of them.

**The hiring math distorts.** When the seat is defined by everything that has to get done, the headcount calculation becomes, "we need to hire fifty people to do all this." When the seat is defined by five outcomes, the calculation is, "who do we need to own these outcomes," and the answer is usually far fewer people doing far more impactful work.

## How to do it

Three moves.

**Define the function before the person.** Wipe the board. Ignore who is currently in the seat. Ask: what is the best structure to get the work of this business done. Name the seat by its function (Sales, Marketing, Operations, Finance) before naming the person who will sit in it.

**Compress the seat to five outcome-bullets.** Start with the long list of everything the seat touches. Then ask the reframing question: what is this seat's *role* in the organization, not what is this seat *responsible for as a list of tasks*. The role is the outcome. Marketing's role is to create leads and maximize brand power. The website, the PR, the photography, the campaigns are mechanisms. Mechanisms are not bullets on the accountability chart.

**Put the right name in the seat using GWC.** Once the seat is defined by outcomes, ask whether the person assigned to it Gets it, Wants it, and has the Capacity to do it. *Gets it* is natural aptitude. A finance person who is bad at math probably does not get the seat. *Wants it* is genuine appetite for the work, not just willingness to do it. *Capacity* is the skill the person has built through training and experience. All three are required.

## What good looks like

A marketing seat defined as:

- Create leads
- Maximize brand power
- Deliver brand-aligned customer experiences
- Run measurable campaigns
- Build the marketing team

The scorecard for this seat has five metrics, not seventeen. The conversation with the marketing leader is, "Your job is to create leads. The website, the PR, the lead nurturing, those are how you go do it. That is up to you. The way I will measure you is whether we are getting leads."

A finance seat defined the same way. An operations seat defined the same way. Every layer of the org defined the same way, from the executive team down to the individual contributor.

When the discipline holds, the question "who is responsible for that?" has a fast, unambiguous answer. The accountability chart becomes the org's source of truth, not a poster on the wall.

## Common mistakes

- **Twelve bullets instead of five.** The person understands their workload but not the why behind it. They cannot bring their creative self to the work because they are running a long list, not owning a small set of outcomes.
- **Outcome-bullets that are secretly task-bullets.** "Generate press by engaging analysts and creating captivating content" is not an outcome. "Maximize press coverage" is the outcome. The first version sneaks the *how* into the role definition. Strip it out.
- **Defining the seat around the person currently in it.** This produces an org chart that matches today's roster, not the structure the business needs. Always design the seats first, then assess whether the person GWCs the seat.
- **Manager grabs the monkey back.** The seat-holder comes to the manager with their problem. The manager solves it for them. Now the manager owns the work. The accountability chart says the seat-holder owns the outcome, so the manager's job is to ask, "how can I help you get there?", not to take the work back. (The canonical reference here is the 1974 HBR article *Management Time: Who's Got the Monkey?*, recommended in the source video.)

## The reframe

The most common organizational friction in a growing business, the toe-stepping, the "I thought you were doing that," the attrition of strong people who feel undefined, is almost never a culture problem. It is an accountability-chart problem. Specifically, it is a seats-defined-by-tasks-instead-of-outcomes problem.

> "Anytime we start to see dysfunction, the very first place that I'm always going to look is the accountability chart."
>
> Rick Fawcett, [Strengthening the Accountability Chart](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=T_ob72LrCp0), 2021

Fix the accountability chart and most of the rest of the dysfunction quietly resolves.
