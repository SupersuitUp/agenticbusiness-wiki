# Go and See, Two Eyes Two Ears

*The leader's most powerful analytical instrument is direct observation of the work. Mystery-shop the funnel, sit at the bottleneck, watch real customers struggle with the real product. The insight arrives faster than any dashboard can deliver it.*

## What it is

Operators default to data. Dashboards, reports, KPIs, A/B-test results. Data is real, but it is slow, filtered, and shaped by whoever built the schema. By the time the leader sees the chart, the answer has been laundered through three layers of interpretation.

Direct observation arrives faster and cleaner. Standing in the warehouse for eight hours. Mystery-shopping the sales funnel. Watching a real customer try to use the product over their shoulder. Walking to the part of the assembly line where inventory is stacking up. The leader's two eyes and two ears are the highest-bandwidth analytical instruments the operator owns.

> "I'm going to introduce you to the most powerful analytics you have as a leader. Your two eyes and your two ears. Use them, because you will get insights so much faster than the data can get to you to give you insights."
>
> — Jon McNeill, recounting an early mentor, 2026-04-09

## Why it works

Direct observation skips the interpretation layer. A dashboard reduces the system to a small number of summary metrics chosen by whoever built it. The summary loses everything that did not fit the schema. The leader watching the work sees what the schema dropped: the worker threading a blind bolt at the wrong angle, the customer giving up at click 47 of 64, the salesperson going through the motions on a callback they do not believe in.

Direct observation also breaks the bystander effect that grows around any chart. Once the bottleneck is visible to the leader, the leader cannot un-see it. The political cost of inaction is no longer abstract.

## How to use it

**Mystery-shop your own funnel before the next board meeting.** Buy your own product. Submit a support ticket. Sign up for the trial under a fake name. McNeill's first move on joining Tesla was test-driving cars at eight stores under different email addresses to find out why nine thousand recent test drives had not been called back.

**Stand where the inventory stacks up.** In a factory, the bottleneck is wherever work-in-progress is piling up. In a software business, the bottleneck is wherever tickets queue, deals stall, or signups drop off. Go stand there.

**Watch a real customer use the product.** Scott Cook called this "follow me home." A senior executive at Intuit sits with an end user doing real work and watches every click. New product ideas come from these sessions more reliably than from any roadmap meeting. Chip Berg's team at Procter and Gamble invented the Swiffer by watching moms mop their kitchens.

**Stay an hour, not five minutes.** Ten minutes in, you will spot the obvious problem and want to fix it. The leader who leaves after fifteen minutes solves only the top-of-mind issue. Staying a full hour reveals the second-order patterns that the worker has stopped noticing because they have lived with them too long.

## Worked example

Tesla, mid-Model-X ramp, was bottlenecked on the Falcon Wing doors. The doors were not fitting. Inventory was piling at the door-installation point on the assembly line. McNeill and Musk walked to the point where the inventory was stacking up. They watched for an hour without speaking to the workers.

Within ten minutes, the pattern was visible. The technicians were threading a bolt blind, at an angle they could not see, and the door was going on crooked as a result. The fix was a jig: a simple maintenance-built bracket that held the bolt at the right angle. The maintenance team built the jig that afternoon. The bottleneck cleared.

The insight was not in any dashboard. No KPI tracked "is the bolt being threaded blind." Data that would have surfaced the problem indirectly, after the fact, would have arrived weeks later as a quality-defect trend. The two-eyes-two-ears insight arrived in ten minutes.

The lineage runs back further. McNeill traces the discipline to a software-business mentor who used to make him sit on customer-support calls, framing the most powerful analytics as the leader's own senses. McNeill traces secret-shopping back to the Sam Walton biography. The pattern shows up across industries because the underlying logic is the same.

## What good looks like

A leader operating under this principle:

- Spends at least one block per week in direct observation, not in meetings about the work
- Has personally tried the product, in the real environment, in the last thirty days
- Knows the name of one frontline worker in each major function
- Can describe what bottleneck the current quarter is fighting in concrete physical terms, not in KPI shorthand
- Translates each observation into a teachable insight the relevant team owner can carry forward

## Common mistakes

- **Asking the team to describe the problem instead of going to see it.** A description is an interpretation. The leader who only hears descriptions only ever solves interpreted problems.
- **The white-coat effect.** Workers do behave differently when the boss is watching. Stay long enough that they forget you are there. Five minutes is performance; one hour is the work.
- **Confusing observation with intervention.** The discipline is to see first, intervene second. Leaders who pull the rip cord at minute ten teach their teams to stop bringing them the work.
- **Reserving observation for crises.** The cadence has to be routine. Observation as crisis-only behavior loses its diagnostic power.
