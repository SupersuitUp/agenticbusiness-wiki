# GWC the Seat

*Every seat-holder must Get it, Want it, and have the Capacity to do it. Three yes-or-no questions. A "no" on any one means the seat is wrong for the person. Skill, comp, ego, and longevity cannot cover the gap.*

## What it is

The accountability chart compresses each seat to [five outcome-bullets](/principles/five-bullets-per-seat). That clarity matters only once the right person is in the seat. GWC is the filter that tells you whether they are.

Three questions. Each one is a binary yes or no.

- **G, Gets it.** Does the person truly understand the role, the culture, the systems, the pace, and how the work comes together. Some people get it. Some people never will. There is no in-between.
- **W, Wants it.** Does the person genuinely want this seat, based on fair compensation and the responsibility. Not "willing to do it." Not "trying to do it." *Wants* it.
- **C, Capacity to do it.** Does the person have the time, intellect, skill, knowledge, and emotional bandwidth to do this seat well. Capacity is not just hours, it is the full envelope of what the role demands.

A "no" on any one means it is the wrong seat. Full stop.

> "When you're evaluating your people, the rating on GWC should be a black-and-white 'yes' or 'no.' ... You must get a 'yes' on all three, or the person is in the wrong seat."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

## Why it matters

The filter exists because the alternative is the slow leak that drains most organizations: somebody is in a seat they should not be in, the work suffers, the manager carries part of the workload to compensate, and nobody names the real problem.

Gino's discovery from thousands of EOS engagements: when a leader has clearly articulated the seat and the person still does not step up, it is almost never effort or motivation. One of G, W, or C is missing. The leader and the seat-holder both end up frustrated. Delegation stops working. The leader does some or all of the seat-holder's work.

> "To reach the next level, you need the people that report to you to be able to take the ball and run with it. When you as their leader or manager clearly articulate the seat (including roles, responsibilities, expectations, and measurables) and present that opportunity, you have created an opening. One of two things will happen as a result: Either they will step up and take charge, or they never will. If they don't, it's because one of G, W, or C is missing."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

GWC names which one and tells you what to do next.

## How to do it

Three moves.

**Score every seat-holder on G, W, and C separately.** Yes or no. No "kind of." No "mostly." No "they're getting there." If you cannot say "yes" with conviction, the answer is "no." Apply the filter to your existing team, not just to new hires. Just because someone has held the seat for two years does not mean they GWC it.

**Diagnose which letter is failing.** Each failure mode has its own fix.

- *Does not get it.* They cannot internalize the role even with coaching. They probably never will. Move them or remove them.
- *Does not want it.* They took the seat for the title, the pay, or because someone needed them to. Find a seat they actually want, or part ways. Motivation cannot be installed.
- *Does not have the capacity.* Bandwidth, skill, or temperament does not match what the role requires. Sometimes capacity can be built (training, time). Most of the time, you do not have the runway.

**Apply the Peter Principle warning.** The most common GWC failure in a growing company is capacity: a great individual contributor is promoted into a role that needs different muscles. They want it, they get the culture, but the new seat exceeds their bandwidth. Catch this early. Do not let "they earned the promotion" override the filter.

> "Capacity means having the time as well as the mental, physical, and emotional capacity to do a job well. Sometimes, a position might require a commitment of 55 hours a week where the person is only willing to commit 40. Sometimes the job requires a certain level of intellect, skill, knowledge, and emotional intelligence, and the person doesn't have that capacity. This is the Peter Principle at its finest, where people are elevated to a level of incompetence."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

## What good looks like

Every seat in the accountability chart has a name in it, and every name has a green "yes / yes / yes" on G, W, C against that specific seat.

When a seat-holder is not delivering, the conversation starts with GWC, not with effort or attitude. The diagnosis is shared, not asserted: here is the seat, here are the five outcome-bullets, here are the three questions, which one is failing. The seat-holder usually knows. They will tell you.

When a hire enters the company, GWC runs alongside core values. Both have to pass. Core values use plus / plus-minus / minus, GWC is strictly yes / no.

## Common mistakes

- **Coaching a "want" problem.** No amount of management can install desire. If the person does not want the seat, they will not provide the spark. Find someone who does.
- **Confusing tenure with capacity.** "She has been here eight years" is not a GWC argument. The seat may have outgrown her. The filter does not care about tenure.
- **Holding a seat for a person you like.** Friendship, history, and gratitude are real but separate concerns. The seat exists to deliver the function's outcomes. If the person does not GWC it, they belong in a different seat or a different company.
- **Soft-marking a "no" as a "yes" to avoid the conversation.** The most common failure mode. The whole point of the filter is the binary. Soft-marking destroys the tool.
- **Skipping the filter for the founder.** Founders often occupy multiple seats early on. As the company grows, GWC applies to them too. The founder who used to GWC the integrator seat may not GWC it at $20M revenue.

## The reframe

The hiring question most operators ask is "is this person an A-player." That question is unanswerable in the abstract. The right question is "does this person Get it, Want it, and have the Capacity to do *this specific seat*."

GWC reframes hiring and seat-assignment from a judgment about the person to a judgment about the fit between the person and the seat. The same person can be a strong "no" in one seat and a strong "yes" in the next one over. Move them.

> "Life is much easier for everyone when you have people around you who genuinely get it, want it, and have the capacity to do it."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007
