# IDS the Issue

*Most teams discuss endlessly, identify rarely, and solve almost nothing. Reverse the ratio. Spend the most time identifying the real problem (the stated problem is almost never the real one). Discussion and solution then take minutes. Identify, Discuss, Solve.*

*Distilled from [Gino Wickman](/mentors/gino-wickman), Traction: Get a Grip on Your Business, 2007. IDS is the canonical EOS Issues Solving Track and the discipline that takes up 60 of the 90 minutes in the weekly [Level 10 Meeting](/principles/the-level-10-meeting).*

## What it is

Three steps. They run in order. Most teams skip the first one.

1. **Identify** the real issue. The stated problem is almost never the real problem. The real one is a layer or two underneath. Pull on it until you find the root.
2. **Discuss.** Everyone says what they need to say about the real issue. Once. No politicking.
3. **Solve.** State the solution as a sentence the room agrees on. The solution usually turns into one or more action items on the To-Do List.

> "The Issues Solving Track always follows the three steps: identify, discuss, and solve. The acronym for the track is IDS. ... From now on, when faced with an issue, you simply 'IDS it.'"
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

## Why it matters

> "When addressing issues, leadership teams spend most of their time discussing the heck out of everything, rarely identifying anything, and hardly ever solving something. It's truly an epidemic within the business world."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

Untrained teams default to a single mode: discuss. They state a problem, debate it from every angle for forty-five minutes, leave feeling productive, and have solved nothing. The next week the same problem is back, dressed differently.

The reason is that the stated problem was a symptom of a deeper issue the team never named. Solving the symptom is satisfying for ten minutes, then the next symptom of the same underlying issue appears, and the cycle starts again. IDS breaks the cycle by spending the disproportionate time at the *identify* step.

> "The time spent identifying the real issue can take far longer than the time used for the second and third steps, and that's okay. ... Sometimes you will spend most of your time identifying the issue. As a result, the Discuss and Solve steps will take just a few minutes because the real issue is now clear."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

When identify is done well, discuss and solve are short.

## How to do it

**Pick the top three issues by priority, not by list order.** Do not start at the top of the Issues List. Decide which three are most important and start with number one. Take them in priority order. Solving the most important issue often makes two or three others disappear because they were symptoms of the same root.

**Identify the real issue (Step 1).** Bat the stated issue back and forth until you reach the root. Be willing to make it personal when the root is a person, this is where trust on the leadership team gets tested. The book's worked example: the stated issue is "John's customers are too demanding." Peel the onion. The real issue is that John is in the wrong seat and does not have the capacity to deliver two-day delivery the customers reasonably expect. Now there is something to solve.

Before you discuss, name the type of issue out loud:

> "There are three types of issues. One is a true problem that has to be solved. The second is information that needs to be communicated and agreed to by the team. The third is an idea or opportunity that needs feedback, brainstorming, insight, and/or a green light from the team."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

Saying "I just want to make sure everyone agrees on the new pricing policy" tells the room this is type 2, not type 1, and the resolution comes faster.

**Discuss (Step 2).** Everyone says everything they need to say. Once. Repeating yourself more than once is politicking, not discussing. Healthy conflict is expected, turf protection is not. Fight for the greater good, not for your department. When the discussion starts to loop, move to solve.

Watch for tangents. From Gino: when someone diverges, call out *"Tangent Alert!"* It is a friendly trigger that resets the conversation. If the tangent is a real issue, add it to the Issues List and IDS it in priority order.

**Solve (Step 3).** Someone states the solution as a sentence. The room agrees with that sentence. The action item goes on the To-Do List with a named owner. Next week's Level 10 confirms it was done.

> "When the issue is completely solved, someone must make the solution statement. ... You'll know after the statement that the problem is solved because you'll hear the sweet sound of agreement in the room. The issue is then owned by someone, it's added to the To-Do List, and it gets done."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

## The 10 commandments of solving issues

From Gino, abridged:

1. Thou shalt not rule by consensus.
2. Thou shalt not be a weenie.
3. Thou shalt be decisive.
4. Thou shalt not rely on secondhand information.
5. Thou shalt fight for the greater good.
6. Thou shalt not try to solve them all.
7. Thou shalt live with it, end it, or change it.
8. Thou shalt choose short-term pain and suffering.
9. Thou shalt enter the danger.
10. Thou shalt take a shot.

Read the full set in *Traction*. Three of them deserve a special call-out:

- **#4: Do not solve issues without all the parties in the room.** If the issue involves people who are not present, schedule the meeting that includes them. Solving by hearsay produces wrong solutions.
- **#7: Live with it, end it, or change it.** There are only three options for any issue. Pick one. If you cannot change it or end it, agree to live with it and stop complaining about it.
- **#9: Enter the danger.** The issue you most want to avoid discussing is the one that most needs IDS. Name it.

## What good looks like

A team that has internalized IDS does five things differently.

- The leadership-team meeting spends sixty of ninety minutes on IDS. Not on reporting, not on discussion of items that are not real issues.
- When a number is off-track on the Scorecard, nobody discusses it in the Scorecard section, it drops cleanly to the Issues List and is solved there in priority order.
- Discussions are short because the real issue has been identified before discussion starts.
- Every solved issue produces a named owner and a To-Do List item. The owner is one person, not a committee.
- Issues that the team would have avoided a year ago get raised in the first ten minutes of the meeting. Entering the danger is normal.

## Common mistakes

- **Discussing before identifying.** The default failure mode. The team launches into discussion of the stated problem without asking "is this the real problem?" Hours of debate, no solution that sticks.
- **Working the Issues List top-to-bottom.** Items at the top are not always the most important. Pick the top three in priority order and solve those first.
- **Solving by consensus.** When the team is split, someone has to decide. If the integrator (or the seat-owner) always defers to majority, decisions stay average. From Jim Collins, quoted in *Traction*: *"no major decision we've studied was ever taken at a point of unanimous agreement."*
- **Letting tangents run.** Tangents are the number one reason discussions go long. A friendly *"Tangent Alert!"* costs nothing and saves the meeting.
- **Skipping the solution statement.** Without a one-sentence solution that the room agrees with, the issue feels resolved but actually is not. Next week the same issue returns.

## The reframe

Most teams think their problem-solving problem is that they lack the right answers. The answers are usually present in the room. The real problem is that they spend almost all of their time discussing the wrong issue. IDS reverses the time allocation: the bulk of the work goes to identifying *which* issue, and the answer reveals itself once the issue is right.

When IDS is the team's default, the team becomes one that solves problems instead of one that talks about them. The shift compounds.
