# Inversion: Magic Before Finance

*Do not start with the business plan or the financials. Start with the magic moment. The financials emerge from the magic, not the other way around.*

## What it is

Most founders, especially those trained in finance, want to start with the spreadsheet. Market size. Unit economics. Revenue forecast. Burn. They model the business before they have seen the magic moment land for one customer.

The inversion: do the opposite. Start with the magic moment. Spend time inside it. Iterate on it. Watch a customer experience it. Then, only then, work backwards into the unit economics, the marketing, the financials.

> "Most people show up with a big idea and a deck, and they try to figure it out. I like to figure out the bricks before I figure out the building. The bricks, for me, is the real magic. The magic is really in that moment with the user. When you see it, the rest is easy, actually. I mean, you're gonna create value. But it's the inversion. I invert everything. I'm not a finance person. I start with the magic. The rest is just patterns."
>
> — Michael B. Ajouz, 2026-05-15

The patterns Ajouz refers to are the operating mechanics every business needs: pricing, sales, marketing, finance, hiring. He is not dismissing them. He is saying they are downstream of the magic. They become tractable once the magic is real.

## Why it matters

A business plan built before the magic has been seen is built on speculation. Every variable in the spreadsheet is an assumption. The assumptions compound. The business gets built on a foundation no one has actually verified.

A business built after the magic has been seen has a different shape. The founder has data. They have watched a customer pay. They have heard the customer's reaction. The spreadsheet, when it gets built, is anchored to specifics rather than guesses.

The inversion is harder than it sounds because finance training is internalized. Founders raised on B-school case studies, on McKinsey decks, on venture-style pitching, default to the spreadsheet-first reflex. The inversion is unlearning that reflex.

## How to invert

Three concrete moves.

**Refuse to build the deck until the magic moment is observable.** Most founders feel naked without a deck. The principle says feel naked until you have something real to put in the deck.

**Park the food truck before signing the lease.** The metaphor from the conversation: ice cream operators who tested with a food truck at a single high-traffic location before committing to a brick-and-mortar lease. The truck reveals the magic moment quickly, cheaply, and reversibly. The lease commits the operator long before the magic is verified.

**When the financials look impossible, suspect the magic is not real.** A founder running a model that requires implausible assumptions to make the business work is often dressing up a missing magic moment. The right diagnosis is not better assumptions. It is to go back and check whether the magic actually happens.

## Worked example

The conversation circled back to this principle repeatedly when discussing the teacher tool, the music business, and a commercial real estate consulting idea. Each time, the founder's instinct was to think strategically about the larger play (a teacher-productivity SaaS, a music label, a real estate advisory firm). Each time, Ajouz redirected to the single moment that would prove the larger play was real.

The teacher tool: not "let's design a teacher-productivity platform with twelve workflows." It is "let's see if Michelle Oberman, the most intense teacher at the school, presses the button on the report card tool and tells her colleagues this changed her life." If that one moment lands, the larger play is real. If it does not, the platform was always speculation.

The music label: not "let's structure equity carve-outs for artists and build a label." It is "if I sign one artist and put them in the game I already own, does the monetization land?" One artist. Real numbers. Then expand.

The real estate advisory: not "let's build a consulting practice for commercial real estate operators." It is "what is the smallest test that proves the advisor's judgment moves the needle on a single deal?"

## What good looks like

A founder operating under this principle:

- Has visible discomfort with abstract planning until the magic is real
- Spends time physically in the magic moment (behind the counter, in the room, at the customer's desk)
- Has a small experiment running that is testing the magic, not the strategy
- Refuses to scale, hire, or raise until the small experiment has produced unmistakable signal
- Talks about the customer's reaction more than they talk about the addressable market

## Common mistakes

- **Believing the deck is the proof.** A polished deck convinces investors. It does not prove the magic. Founders who optimize for deck quality before magic quality end up funded but failing.
- **Skipping the magic because it feels too small.** The magic is one customer at one moment. To a founder ambitious about scale, this can feel beneath them. That feeling is the trap.
- **Treating the inversion as a lifelong practice.** Inversion is a starting discipline. Once the magic is real, the operator has to learn the patterns (finance, sales, marketing) and apply them. The inversion is not a permanent allergy to financial discipline.
