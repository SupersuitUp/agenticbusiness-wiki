# Missionary and Mercenary

*Care about the work like a missionary. Run the business like an operator. Neither alone holds up.*

## What it is

Two postures founders take. Both fail in isolation.

The missionary cares about the work, the customer, the mission. They are willing to put in years for outcomes the cynical bystander would not understand. They love what they are doing. But missionaries who are not also operators get destroyed by economic reality. Their cause is good. Their business runs out of money. Their followers scatter.

The mercenary runs the business well. They know the numbers, the margins, the pricing, the levers. They optimize for return. But mercenaries who are not also missionaries get found out. Their products lack soul. Their customers feel extracted from. The world is good at sensing the absence of care, and the basis of competition rewards those who actually care.

The principle: be both. Care like a missionary about the work and the people. Operate like a mercenary about the business. Both at once. The integration is what produces durable wins.

> "If you're a fucking mercenary, right? Maybe, but you're not gonna love it, and you're gonna be found out. The world is gonna have two pounds and it's gonna blow you out. The missionary that knows technology, and also is willing to learn the mercenary business, that is the serpent, innocent of the dove. That's scripture."
>
> — Michael B. Ajouz, 2026-05-15

## Why it matters

Now more than at most points in the last century, the missionary-and-mercenary integration matters specifically because AI is going to commoditize everything that does not have soul. The mercenary who is good at the business but does not care about the work will lose to the missionary-mercenary who is good at the business and also cares. The pure missionary who cannot run a business will lose to the missionary-mercenary who has learned to.

> "The world is gonna have two pounds. AI is gonna sort them. People that can actually do and change things, that's you guys. That's what AI's gonna sort. The world's gonna have two ponds. So people that can actually do and change things, those people will be valuable."
>
> — Michael B. Ajouz, 2026-05-15

## How to be both

Three concrete moves.

**Pick the work missionarily.** The first question is not "what is profitable" but "what do you actually care about." If you are building in an industry you do not love, you will be out-loved by an operator who does. Education, music, healthcare, finance, real estate. Whatever the domain, pick one you would care about even if it did not pay well. Then make sure it pays well.

**Operate the business mercenarily.** Pricing, margins, capital structure, leverage, team composition, cash flow. These are not betrayals of the mission. They are the substrate that lets the mission keep operating. The missionary who refuses to think about them ends up out of business and unable to serve the mission anymore.

**Be willing to learn the side you are weak on.** Most founders have a natural pull toward one. Engineers and artists toward missionary. Finance people toward mercenary. The principle requires deliberate effort to develop the side that does not come naturally. This is not optional.

## Worked examples

From the conversation:

Ajouz's nonstandard auto insurance business: the missionary side is that the underserved community he serves is getting ripped off by the existing operators. He cares about giving them a better option. The mercenary side is that the unit economics work, the basis of competition is low, the equity compounds, and the financial returns are substantial. Both at once.

A music business chasing artists: the missionary side is loving the artists, caring about their careers, building a platform where they can win. The mercenary side is monetization mechanics (merchandise, concerts, IP licensing, video games) where the math actually delivers margin. Without the missionary side, the artists do not sign. Without the mercenary side, the business does not last.

A teacher productivity tool: the missionary side is loving the teachers, taking their pain seriously, building tools that make their work better. The mercenary side is the unit economics of school district sales, the structure of equity in the business, the path to scale across IB-curriculum schools nationally. Both.

## What good looks like

A founder operating as both:

- Can describe what they love about the work in a way that lands on the customer
- Can also describe the unit economics and the margin structure without flinching
- Spends time with the customer (missionary) and with the spreadsheet (mercenary)
- Will not let a price be set that is below the cost to deliver, even when the customer pulls at heartstrings
- Will not let a price be set that extracts value from the customer in a way the customer would resent if they understood it

## Common mistakes

- **Missionary collapse into mercenary.** A founder who started loving the work, hits financial pressure, and starts cutting corners on the part of the work they used to love. The customer feels it. The business stops compounding.
- **Mercenary collapse into mission-washing.** A founder who is running a financial extraction business but markets it with missionary language. Customers see through this. Long-term, the brand is worth less than the missionary-mercenary brand built honestly.
- **Missionary refusing to learn mercenary.** "I don't want to think about money." This kills the mission as surely as the inverse. The mission dies when the business that funds it dies.
