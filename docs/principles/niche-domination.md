# Niche Domination

*The coral reef versus the blue ocean. Most of the life is in the small niches because the big fish cannot fit. Wildly profitable small businesses beat scrappy positions in crowded markets.*

## What it is

The strategic principle: pick a market small enough that the big players will not enter it, that you can dominate cleanly, and that produces durable cash flow because the basis of competition is low. Reject the instinct to chase large crowded markets where the basis of competition is intense and the average return on effort approaches zero.

The metaphor:

> "If you look at an ocean, most of the ocean is blue ocean. Big water. All the fish are super big. There's not that much life. All the life in the ocean is actually in the coral reefs. Because it's protected. Big fish can't fit in there. That's where all the life is."
>
> — Michael B. Ajouz, 2026-05-15

A wildly profitable small business in a niche the big players cannot reach beats a heroic position in a market where eight well-funded teams are running at the same target. The math compounds: the big crowded market splits its addressable revenue across many competitors, each driving the basis of competition down. The niche, dominated, captures the revenue cleanly and the operator gets to keep it.

## Why it matters now

The AI moment makes this sharper, not softer. Two reasons.

The first reason is that AI-enabled small operations can run with the unit economics of much larger businesses. A two-person shop can deliver what a thirty-person shop used to. So the niche that was previously sub-scale for a serious operator is now serviceable.

The second reason is that the big players are paying attention to the big markets. They are not paying attention to the coral reef. The Hispanic nonstandard auto insurance market. The IB-curriculum K-12 school. The clothing manufacturer worried about agent-readable supply data. These are not on the radar of the firms with $20 billion in assets.

> "Today, I can have a $10 million SaaS business that generates $7 million in cash flow. And it takes no time. And nobody's gonna go after it, because it just sits there. Because it's not competitive. Because it's not big enough. Small mountains, wildly profitable."
>
> — Michael B. Ajouz, 2026-05-15

## How to find your niche

Three filters.

**The basis of competition is low.** The operators you would compete against are not institutional. They are smaller, less sophisticated, less well-funded. You can out-think them at unit economics, at product quality, at customer experience. If the competition is sharp and well-funded, the niche is not the right one.

**The customer is being underserved.** There is a real pain that the existing options are not addressing well. The customers know it. They are looking for something better but the options on the table are weak. Your job becomes building the better option, not creating the demand.

**You have a credible reason to be there.** You have an insider, a relationship, a domain advantage, a personal story that makes your presence in this niche legible. Without that, you are just another outsider trying to break in.

The example from the conversation: the operator's nonstandard auto insurance business serving the Hispanic, often unbanked, often unrepresented driver community. Big insurance carriers will not touch it because of fraud risk. The operator has an insider partner from the community. The customers are getting ripped off by the existing local players. The operator builds a better option, captures the customer, retains them as they move into standard insurance, then sells the operational platform to compete with the dinosaurs.

## What good looks like

A founder who has correctly identified a niche to dominate can:

- Describe the size of the market in dollars, with a credible source
- Name the three to seven competitors who already exist in it, and explain why they are weak
- Explain why a much larger operator has not entered, and why they probably will not
- Identify the insider relationship, domain advantage, or unique angle they bring
- Describe the unit economics of one customer in detail

If the founder cannot do all five, the niche has not been chosen. The right next step is to keep looking.

## Common mistakes

- **Chasing the largest available market.** The big market looks attractive because the addressable revenue is large. But the competition is sharp and well-funded, the basis of competition is brutal, and the unit economics are squeezed. The math does not work out for the small operator.
- **Treating "niche" as a stepping stone to the larger market.** The niche is not a beachhead. It is the durable business. Operators who treat it as a stepping stone tend to underinvest in dominating it and overinvest in the speculative expansion.
- **Picking a niche the operator does not actually care about.** The niche has to be one the operator is willing to live in for years. If the founder does not love the customers, will not enjoy learning the unique structure of the market, and treats it as just a financial opportunity, the operation will not produce the durable wins.
