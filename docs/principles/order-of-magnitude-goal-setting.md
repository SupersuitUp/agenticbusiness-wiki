# Order of Magnitude Goal Setting

*If you set a five percent improvement goal, you will get three percent. If you set a 10x goal, your team is forced to question the business model itself. The goal is the lever.*

## What it is

Most operators set incremental goals. Improve conversion by ten percent. Cut cycle time by twenty percent. Add fifteen percent to revenue. The incremental goal looks ambitious on paper, but it is solvable by tweaking the status quo: a price test, a copy change, one more salesperson, a tighter SLA. The team works hard, the team hits the goal, and the business model never gets touched.

The order-of-magnitude goal works differently. 10x. 100x. 20x. A goal that big is unsolvable by tweaks. The only path to it is reinvention: the team has to question the variables it has been treating as fixed, including the business model itself. Reinvention is what produces the breakout.

> "Part of the secret to Elon's secret sauce is he sets these order of magnitude improvement goals. So 10x, 100x. He came to me and said, 'This is your goal. Improve digital sales by 20x.' So now it's like, 'Oh crap, I got to think way differently about this.'"
>
> — Jon McNeill, 2026-04-09

## Why it works

A five to seven percent goal lets the team optimize the existing system. A 10x goal forces them to ask which parts of the existing system they should keep and which they should throw out. The math of incremental improvement does not get there. The team has to rebuild from a different starting point, and the rebuild is where the new economics emerge.

The goal also changes who participates. Incremental goals belong to whichever team owns the metric. Order-of-magnitude goals are too big for one team and pull in engineering, manufacturing, finance, and product. The reinvention happens across functions because no single function can solve the gap alone.

## How to use it

**Name a goal that is uncomfortable to say out loud.** If the goal feels reasonable, it is not yet an order-of-magnitude goal. The discomfort is the signal that the team will be forced to think differently.

**Pair the goal with a concrete reference point.** Tesla's online-sales work was anchored to Domino's pizza: 64 clicks to buy a car, 10 thumb taps to buy a pizza, the goal is 10. The reference makes the goal real and provides a model to study.

**Be willing to drop the religion.** Tesla's 360,000 build-to-order car configurations were a foundational tenet of the brand. When the data showed customers were really buying two configurations, McNeill's team killed the religion. Order-of-magnitude goals usually require killing at least one piece of religion. Naming the religion in advance, and pre-committing to drop it if the data demands, is part of the discipline.

**Watch for second-order benefits.** When Tesla collapsed to two configurations, manufacturing simplified, the supply chain simplified, engineering time redirected to features that mattered, and the customer's decision fatigue dropped. The order-of-magnitude goal forced the simplification; the simplification produced downstream wins no one had planned for.

## Worked example

Tesla's digital sales challenge was framed by Musk to McNeill as a 20x goal: take online sales from a tiny fraction of revenue to a number that would land the quarter. The team ran the data on the current funnel. Sixty-four clicks to buy a car. Three hundred sixty thousand build combinations. Two configurations actually being bought. The order-of-magnitude goal was unsolvable inside the existing model.

The team collapsed configurations to two. Cut clicks dramatically. The fear was that Tesla would look like Honda. The reality was that Tesla had software on wheels and a different product category, and the simplification accelerated everything: the factory, the supply chain, engineering throughput, and the consumer's experience.

The lesson generalizes. Every order-of-magnitude goal McNeill describes in *The Algorithm* follows the same pattern. The big number forces a question about the current model. The current model has a piece of religion in it. The religion gets dropped. The new model is better in ways no one fully predicted.

## What good looks like

An operator using this principle:

- Picks one or two metrics per year and sets order-of-magnitude goals against them
- Pairs each goal with a concrete reference point from outside the industry
- Names the existing religion the team will need to question
- Lets the goal pull in cross-functional teams rather than assigning it to a single owner
- Measures progress against the order-of-magnitude target, not against incremental milestones

## Common mistakes

- **Setting an order-of-magnitude goal without permission to question the business model.** If the team is told to 20x conversion but not allowed to touch pricing, packaging, or the product, the goal is a setup for failure. The whole point is to unlock model-level changes.
- **Setting too many at once.** A team can run one or two order-of-magnitude goals at a time. Five becomes background noise.
- **Confusing aspirational vision with operational target.** Order-of-magnitude goals have owners, timelines, and measurable progress. A wall poster does not count.
