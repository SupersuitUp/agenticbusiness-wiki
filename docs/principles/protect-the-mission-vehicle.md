# Protect the Mission Vehicle

*If you are sufficiently ambitious, the business that carries your mission is a target. Protect it from predators outside, and from temptations inside.*

---

*Sourced from [Eric Ries](/mentors/eric-ries), Incorruptible: Why Good Companies Go Bad... And How Great Companies Stay Great (Authors Equity, May 2026).*

## Stub

This page is a stub. The primary source is Eric Ries' Incorruptible, and the right move for any founder running an ambitious business is to read the book directly. What follows is a compressed pointer to the principles most relevant to building an agentic business. Enough to get them on your radar. Not a substitute for the source.

## What it is

Every business large enough to throw off real cash flow becomes a target. The predators always show up. The question is whether the founders built the vehicle to survive them.

There are two attack surfaces. Both kill missions if ignored.

**External predators.** Private equity firms, hostile boards, hedge funds, the "best practices" coalition that shows up the night before something important and offers a deal you cannot refuse. Sol Price founded FedMart, built it into a forty-store retail innovator with $350M in annual sales, and was voted out of his own company in 1975 by investors who thought capping margins and paying workers double the prevailing wage was leaving money on the table. They got what they wanted. By 1982 FedMart was liquidated. Ries calls this the mystery of the golden goose: sophisticated investors who killed the very thing that made the company valuable.

**Internal temptations.** The founder's own drift. The partner who starts cutting corners under financial pressure. The IPO that quietly rewires what the company optimizes for. The professional CEO brought in to "scale" who replaces the mission with the stock price. Most founders assume their own values will protect the company. They are wrong. Ries spends a chapter on this in his own voice, including the night he was on the bathroom floor at 3:15am after a hedge-fund-and-governance-experts coalition offered LTSE a deal his team had to refuse to survive. Success and good intentions are not enough.

The principle: the vehicle carrying the mission is itself a designed object. If the design is weak, the vehicle gets captured. If the design is strong, the mission survives the founders.

## The two-part blueprint

Paraphrasing Ries' core blueprint from Incorruptible:

**Create something worth protecting.** A mission aligned with human flourishing, a principled ethos that lives in every decision, customers and employees who trust the firm because the firm has earned it. Without this, there is nothing to protect. Mission-washing does not count.

**Build with structural integrity.** Governance, ownership, voting rights, board composition, the corporate form itself. The more successful the company, the bigger the target. Standard corporate structures buckle under the gravity of the financial system. Costco's distinctive practices held because they were defended by a board structure that could refuse the "best practice" pressure. Patagonia's mission held because Yvon Chouinard rebuilt the ownership structure to make capture mathematically impossible.

Both parts. Neither half works alone. Everything worth protecting will eventually need protection.

## Handles to look at

For an agentic business specifically, the handles to look at first:

- **Ownership structure.** Who actually holds the equity, and what claim do they have on the mission. Default cap tables are designed for liquidity, not for mission durability.
- **Board composition.** Who is on the board, what are they accountable to, and what can they force. The default board is investor-controlled. Mission-controlled is a different shape entirely.
- **Voting rights.** Dual-class shares, mission-protective provisions, and similar mechanisms exist for a reason. The reason is that whoever holds the votes decides what the company becomes.
- **Funding source choice.** Every dollar comes attached to a worldview. PE dollars carry PE expectations. Strategic dollars carry strategic expectations. Mission-aligned dollars carry mission-aligned expectations. Choose accordingly.
- **Founder accountability.** The founder is part of the threat model. Build the structures that protect the company from the founder's own future weakness, not just from external attackers.

These are not finance-team details. They are the substrate that determines whether the mission survives contact with success.

## Why this matters now

AI compresses the timeline. A founder building an agentic business can reach scale fast enough that capture pressure arrives earlier than past generations encountered it, with less time to design defenses. The default outcome of a mission-driven business that scales without governance discipline is enshittification, founder ouster, or both.

Ries' frame: the corruption of successful companies is as natural and inevitable as gravity. Mission-controlled companies are the exceptions, and they are exceptions because they were designed to be.
