## The challenge underneath it

Hormozi frames the whole thing as a challenge. Define the input-output equation that gets you closer to where you want to go, then do ten times more of the input.

> "Define the input-output equation that gets you closer to where you want to go. And then 10x the input."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-05-31

Most operators do the right activity at the wrong volume. They work out five minutes a week and wonder why nothing moves. In fitness people have enough context to feel the gap. In business they do not, so they think in doubles when the answer is orders of magnitude. If you want a hundred times the output, you need a hundred times the input. (See [Order of Magnitude Goal Setting](/principles/order-of-magnitude-goal-setting) for the same arithmetic applied to goals rather than effort.)

The trap is hidden in the math. You cannot personally produce a hundred times more input with two hands and a calendar. The instant the input has to scale past you, the real principle shows up.

> "If you're trying to do 100 times more, it's probably not just you."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-05-31

## What it is

Leverage is the multiplier that sits between your input and your output. Hormozi describes three levels of it on a single task, cold calls:

1. You make 200 cold calls a day and get a certain number of sales. That is self-employment.
2. You make 200 calls a day until you find someone to make those calls for you. Now you get the same sales while doing nothing. That is one layer of leverage.
3. You make 200 calls a day until you find a recruiter. The recruiter brings you a new caller every week. Every week another 200 calls a day stack on top of the last. One week of your own effort buys a compounding stream of output. That is leverage on leverage.

Each step up the ladder buys output by relinquishing a job you used to do yourself. The mechanism of leverage is not effort. It is letting go.

> "What you unlock in the game of entrepreneurship is leverage. And the way that you unlock leverage is relinquishing control."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-05-31

## Why it works

The thing that got you to quit your job is the thing you now have to stop doing. You felt out of control working for someone else, so you went self-employed to take complete control: you hunt, you kill, you produce, you do everything. That total control was rewarded with money and a life on your terms, which is exactly why it is so hard to give up. The rest of the journey is unlearning the behavior you were most rewarded for.

> "The tough part about the entrepreneurial journey is that the thing that got you to quit your job is the very thing that you have to stop doing."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-05-31

The reason it has to be unlearned in order is that each job you hold onto caps the output of everything downstream of it. As long as you are the one doing delivery, delivery cannot grow past your hours. The same is true of admin, sales, marketing, and management. Control feels like safety and behaves like a ceiling.

## The ladder of relinquishment

Hormozi lays out the sequence an operator gives up, roughly in order:

1. **Production / delivery** on the back end.
2. **Admin work.**
3. **Sales.**
4. **Marketing.**
5. **Management.**
6. **Leadership.**
7. **Financing.**

And then the last one, the one most operators never reach, because it is not a task. It is an identity.

> "Eventually you're like, 'But I'm not needed.' And then you have to give up the desire to be needed by your business. Because many people make their business to fulfill personal desires. But the business doesn't need to do that. It doesn't exist for you. It exists for the customer. You're not required. You think you are because it makes you feel more important."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-05-31

The business exists for the customer. The desire to be needed by it is a personal want wearing an operational disguise, and it is the final piece of control to relinquish.

## What this means for an agentic business

The agentic business is the fastest path up this ladder anyone has ever had, and it tests the principle harder than any previous one.

In Hormozi's example the leverage is people: a caller, a recruiter, a stacking team. Each layer takes weeks to recruit and train. Agents collapse that timeline. A skill that does the input-output activity, pointed at the right context, is the caller, the recruiter, and the stacking team compressed into a workflow you can run today. The 100x input that "is probably not just you" becomes 100x input that is mostly agents.

That makes the relinquishment both easier and harder. Easier, because handing a clearly specified job to an agent costs you minutes, not a hiring cycle. Harder, because the operator who cannot let go of doing the work will hover over every agent output, re-do it by hand, and keep the human ceiling firmly in place while telling himself he is being careful. The agentic operator has to walk the same ladder: give up production to agents, then admin, then the drafting work inside sales and marketing, then the coordination work of management, and finally the desire to be the irreplaceable person in the loop.

The agents do the input. Your job is to define the input-output equation, point the leverage at it, and relinquish control function by function until the business runs for the customer without needing you in the chair.

## What good looks like

An operator using this principle:

- Names the one input-output equation that moves them toward the goal, and states the input volume honestly in orders of magnitude rather than percentages.
- Treats every job they personally do as a candidate to hand off, to a person or an agent, and knows which rung of the ladder they are currently stuck on.
- Builds leverage that stacks (a recruiter who brings callers, an agent that spawns sub-agents) rather than leverage that has to be re-bought every cycle.
- Can describe what the business does when they are not in the room, and is unbothered by the answer.

## Common mistakes

- **Doubling when the answer is 10x or 100x.** Thinking incrementally keeps you doing the right activity at a volume that cannot move the number.
- **Buying leverage and then supervising it back into your own hands.** Re-doing the agent's or the hire's work by hand reinstates the ceiling you paid to remove.
- **Stopping at the financing rung.** Most operators relinquish tasks but keep the identity of being needed, and that identity quietly caps the business at the size of one person's ego.
- **Relinquishing control without first defining the input-output equation.** Handing off work you have not made legible just exports the confusion to whoever, or whatever, you hand it to.
