# Rocks Before Sand

*Every quarter, the leadership team commits to three to seven Rocks (specific, measurable, time-bound priorities, each owned by one person). No new priorities get added mid-quarter. The Rocks go in the jar first, everything else fills in around them.*

*Distilled from [Gino Wickman](/mentors/gino-wickman), Traction: Get a Grip on Your Business, 2007. Rocks are the 90-day execution discipline of EOS, drawn from Stephen Covey's First Things First jar metaphor by way of Verne Harnish's Rockefeller Habits.*

## What it is

The 90-day priority is the longest horizon a human team can hold in focus without drifting. Quarterly Rocks turn that fact into a discipline.

Once per quarter, the leadership team meets for a full day, reviews the vision, and decides on three to seven Rocks for the next 90 days. Each Rock is:

- **Specific.** "Close three core accounts" or "Hire a new controller." Not "improve sales" or "work on the customer service process."
- **Measurable.** At the end of the quarter, the answer is binary: done or not done. No ambiguity.
- **Time-bound.** Due by the last day of the quarter (March 31, June 30, September 30, December 31).
- **Owned by one person.** Exactly one name. Not two. Not a committee. The owner drives it to completion.

Once the Rocks are set, they are locked. No new priorities can be added mid-quarter, no matter how genius the new idea feels.

> "With that, a wall goes up, and no one is allowed to throw anything else over it, whether it's a genius-level new idea or a hand grenade. Once the priorities are set for this quarter, no new priorities can be added! ... New ideas and thoughts that arise during the quarter should be put on the V/TO Issues List for next quarter."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

## Why it matters

Gino takes the term Rock from Stephen Covey's jar metaphor, by way of Verne Harnish.

> "Picture a glass cylinder set on a table. Next to the cylinder are rocks, gravel, sand, and a glass of water. ... If you, as most people do, pour the water in first, the sand in second, the gravel in third, and the rocks last, what happens? Those big priorities won't fit inside the glass cylinder. That's your typical day."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

When everything is a priority, nothing is. Most growing companies operate that way by default: the whole leadership team is fighting for resources, every idea feels urgent, every initiative is "important." Twelve months later, very little has actually shipped.

The 90-Day World inverts this. By committing to a small set of named priorities every quarter, the team stops drifting and starts compounding. The book documents Image One (a 35-person operator) seeing 35 people times 2 Rocks times 4 quarters = 280 directional moves in a single year. That is the magnitude shift.

> "When everything is important, nothing is important. The way you move the company forward is one 90-day period at a time."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

## How to do it

Six steps, lightly summarized from Gino's full process.

**Step 1: List everything that needs to get done this quarter.** Whiteboard exercise. Brain dump as a leadership team. Most teams produce 10 to 20 candidates. One client of Gino's produced 75.

**Step 2: Keep, kill, or combine.** Walk the list. For each candidate, decide: is this a true 90-day priority, or is it noise. Make as many passes as needed until the list is down to three to seven Rocks. The right ones surface naturally when the discipline holds.

**Step 3: Set the due date and define each Rock crisply.** Due date is the end of the quarter. Each Rock must be specific and measurable. *"Close $1 million in new business."* *"Document delivery process and train all."* *"Narrow CFO candidates to two."* Not *"work on sales."*

**Step 4: Assign one owner per Rock.** Exactly one. Vague co-ownership kills accountability. The owner builds the timeline, calls the meetings, pushes the people.

**Step 5: Cascade to leadership team Rocks.** After the company Rocks are set, each leadership-team member picks their own three to seven Rocks. Company Rocks they own roll into their list automatically. The remaining slots fill with what is most critical to their function.

**Step 6: Build the Rock Sheet.** One landscaped page. Company Rocks at the top, each leader's individual Rocks underneath. This sheet is brought into every [Level 10 Meeting](/principles/the-level-10-meeting) and reviewed in the Rock Review section.

The leadership team should master Rocks on themselves for two quarters before rolling them out to the rest of the organization.

> "It takes two quarters to master Rocks. You will not master the process the first time around. Be patient, because true mastery comes from experience."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

## What good looks like

A leadership team running Rocks well has:

- A Rock Sheet on one page, visible in every Level 10 Meeting
- Three to seven company Rocks for the quarter, each with a single owner
- Each leadership-team member with their own three to seven Rocks
- No new "priorities" added mid-quarter (new ideas go on the V/TO Issues List for the next quarterly meeting)
- An 80 percent or better completion rate at the end of each quarter (100 percent is perfectionist and unrealistic)
- A quarterly off-site that opens with reviewing the Rocks just completed, named done or not done, before setting the next quarter's

## Common mistakes

- **Too many Rocks.** More than seven is no longer a focusing exercise. It is the old "everything is a priority" pattern with a new label. Three to seven is the discipline, with three to four being the sweet spot.
- **Vague Rocks.** "Improve customer satisfaction" is not a Rock. "Raise average customer rating to a 9" is a Rock. If the team will argue at quarter-end about whether it was done, it was not specific enough.
- **Co-owned Rocks.** When two people own a Rock, no one owns it. Pick one.
- **Adding mid-quarter Rocks.** The whole point of the wall is that it does not move. Park new ideas on the V/TO Issues List and revisit at the next quarterly.
- **Skipping the Level 10 Rock Review.** If the Rocks live in a binder no one opens until quarter-end, they are not driving behavior. The weekly on-track or off-track read-out is what produces traction.
- **Commitment fizzle.** Starting the Rocks process with energy and then letting it drift after two quarters. The team starts to read Rocks as "another flavor of the month." Rocks compound only when the routine is sacred.

## The reframe

Most operators describe their execution problem as "we don't have time to do the important things." The actual problem is that they have not named what the important things are for this quarter, in a form a person can be accountable to. Rocks are that form.

Less is more. The leadership team that ships three things every quarter for four quarters has shipped twelve. The leadership team that "is working on" thirty things across the same period has shipped two or three.
