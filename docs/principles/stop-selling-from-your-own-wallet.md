# Stop Selling From Your Own Wallet

*You price your offer for the version of you who cannot afford it. Price it for the customer who has the money, and stop taking money advice from people who have none.*

## The trap

Most operators set their price by asking a silent question: would I pay this? The answer comes back from their own bank account and from the friends and family they run the number past. If those people are broke, the price comes out broke.

> "Why would you listen to the people who don't have money on how to get money? They don't know where it is. They don't know how to get it. And more specifically, they don't know how to serve the people who've got it."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-06-01

Hormozi's reframe is to price as if the buyer is rich, because a large share of them are. The top ten percent of Americans hold a million dollars or more in net worth: one in ten people.

> "Forever imagine that everyone is rich. So here's the reality that will shock you. That top 10% of Americans have million dollar plus net worth. One in 10 people."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-06-01

The money exists. The operator who stays stuck is usually not failing to find buyers with means. They are selling those buyers something the buyers do not want, at a price that pre-disqualifies the operator from being taken seriously.

## Too cheap to be believed

Underpricing reads as a quality signal, and the signal it sends is bad. A price far below the promise tells a serious buyer the promise is not real. Hormozi describes a health company he believed was mispriced. He pushed for double, settled for a fifty percent raise after a long fight, and watched the close rate move the opposite direction from the one the operator feared.

> "We raised the price by 50%. That's a lot. What do you think it did to the close rates? They went up. They were so cheap compared to the promise and what they were delivering that people didn't even believe that it worked."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-06-01

Price is part of the offer's claim about itself. Set too low against the value delivered, it undercuts the claim. This is the inverse of the usual operator fear that every dollar of price costs a point of conversion.

## The anchor: five to ten times the price

The mechanism for breaking out of own-wallet pricing is an anchor: an upsell priced five to ten times above the core offer, built to be worth it. The instruction has two halves, and the second half is the discipline.

> "Whatever your upsell is, five to 10X the price. And then just make sure it's something that you'd be happy to deliver for five to 10 times the price."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-06-01

When operators object that a ten-times offer would be ten times the work, Hormozi points at the two variables they actually control.

> "We have value and we have price. Move one of them. Either do less or charge more. I would encourage you to just charge more."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-06-01

Run the arithmetic on the anchor and the margin usually surprises the operator: the thing that would let you charge a price with an extra zero on it often costs only a few percent of that price to deliver. And even when nobody buys the anchor, it pays for itself by reframing everything cheaper.

> "This is called an anchor for a reason. If no one buys it, no big deal. But the good news is that it'll still help you sell the rest of everyone else at a higher percentage and even at a higher rate because it'll look like a good deal in comparison."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-06-01

## Expect more no's

Pricing for the buyer who has money means pricing past the buyer who does not, and most people you talk to do not. The metric to optimize is dollars, not agreement.

> "You should expect only one in five or one in 10 people to say yes. Expect more no's. And this is the sweet spot of making money. The sweet spot isn't the most yes's, it's the most money. And that is never with the most yes's."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-06-01

The right customer, when you reach them, will often agree to a number that stuns the operator, because the number is large only to the operator. The buyer is standing somewhere else.

> "It's only that much money to you because you still live here. If you sell to rich people long enough, they will make you one of them."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-06-01

## Absolute profit, not relative

The deepest version of the principle is a unit shift. Operators chase percentages and quantities when the only thing that matters is the absolute dollars of profit that land in the business.

> "A single person paying ten thousand dollars for something that cost two thousand dollars is the same as four hundred people buying a fifty dollar thing that cost twenty-five dollars. These are the same. So do not underestimate the power of large prices and small quantities."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-06-01

The mismatch costs more than it looks. Selling a small thing to a big buyer leaves the large, disproportionately profitable dollars on the table.

> "The only thing worse than selling a $1,000 thing to a $100 buyer is selling a hundred dollar thing to a thousand dollar buyer. In the first you lose a hundred bucks. In the second you lose nine hundred."
>
> — Alex Hormozi, 2026-06-01

This is the same arithmetic that sits under [Lifetime Gross Profit](/concepts/lifetime-gross-profit): raising price is the first and highest-leverage way to grow the gross profit a customer produces, and a few high-margin customers can carry a business that four hundred low-margin ones would only exhaust.

## What this means for an agentic business

The agentic business is built for large prices and small quantities, because agents collapse the variable cost of delivery. When the marginal cost of serving one more high-end customer is a workflow run rather than a new hire, the "do less or charge more" choice tilts hard toward "charge more" with no penalty in delivery quality. The premium anchor that costs a few percent to fulfill costs even less when agents do the fulfilling.

That is the opportunity and the trap. The opportunity is that an operator with strong [leverage](/principles/relinquish-control-for-leverage) can serve the one-in-ten buyer at a margin that compounds, and let the agents absorb the work that would otherwise make a ten-times offer ten times the labor. The trap is that agents make it cheap to chase volume too, and an operator selling from his own wallet will point that leverage at four hundred fifty-dollar customers who are the biggest drain on the business. The same agentic stack can manufacture either outcome. The pricing discipline decides which.

Price the offer for the customer who has the money. Build the anchor you would be happy to deliver. Let the agents make the high-margin delivery cheap, and count absolute profit, not agreement.

## What good looks like

An operator using this principle:

- States their price by reference to the value delivered and the buyer who has money, never by reference to what they themselves could afford.
- Carries an anchor offer priced five to ten times the core, built to be worth it, and knows its real delivery cost as a percentage of price.
- Treats a low yes-rate on the premium tier as a sign they are reaching the right buyer, not a sign the price is wrong.
- Optimizes for absolute profit dollars and is willing to serve fewer, higher-paying customers to get them.

## Common mistakes

- **Pricing from your own bank account.** The operator's affordability is irrelevant to the buyer's. Pricing from it imports the operator's constraints into the customer's decision.
- **Taking pricing advice from broke advisors.** Friends and family who do not have money do not know how to serve people who do. Their feedback on price is feedback from the wrong avatar.
- **Reading a low close rate as a pricing failure.** On a premium anchor, most people saying no is the design, not the defect. The number to watch is total profit.
- **Selling small to big buyers.** A hundred-dollar offer to a thousand-dollar buyer forfeits the nine hundred dollars of mostly-profit the buyer was ready to spend.
- **Using agentic leverage to chase volume.** Cheap delivery makes it tempting to win four hundred low-margin customers when the same stack could serve a handful at far higher absolute profit.
