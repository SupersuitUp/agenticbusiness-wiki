# The Level 10 Meeting

*The leadership team meets weekly, same day, same time, same one-page agenda. Ninety minutes. Sixty of those minutes belong to solving issues. Start on time, end on time, rate the meeting an 8 or higher every week.*

## What it is

Most leadership meetings are unstructured rituals that drift, run long, and produce nothing actionable. People walk out unsure what was decided or who owns what. The Level 10 Meeting is the inverse: a tightly structured 90-minute weekly meeting that the leadership team holds without exception, with the same seven-section agenda every week, and that gets rated by every attendee at the end. The target rating is 10. The minimum acceptable is 8.

The agenda is the meeting. Once you have it, you do not reinvent it.

> "The Level 10 Meeting was developed as a result of being engaged by a number of clients to improve their meetings. The guiding principles are based on human nature. This agenda was developed through real-world trial and error and experimentation with many different methodologies. Today, all EOS clients follow this exact agenda."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

## The agenda

Ninety minutes. One page. Seven sections, in this exact order.

| Section | Min | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| **Segue** | 5 | Each person shares a piece of good news (personal or business). Transition from working *in* the business to working *on* it. Phones off. |
| **Scorecard** | 5 | Review the 5 to 15 numbers that matter. Each is reported as on-track or off-track. Any off-track number is dropped to the IDS list. *No discussion here.* |
| **Rock review** | 5 | Each Rock (90-day priority) is reported as on-track or off-track. Same discipline: off-track Rocks go to IDS. *No discussion here.* |
| **Customer / employee headlines** | 5 | Short headlines about customer or employee news, good or bad. Bad news goes to IDS. |
| **To-Do List** | 5 | Review every commitment from last week's meeting. Done or not done. Strike the done ones. Items lingering two weeks get escalated. |
| **IDS** | 60 | Solve the top three issues using the [Identify, Discuss, Solve track](/principles/ids-the-issue). This is where the meeting pays for itself. |
| **Conclude** | 5 | Restate the new To-Do List. Decide what gets communicated to the org. Every attendee rates the meeting 1 to 10. Target an 8 or better. |

The first six sections all feed the seventh. Anything that needs discussion in any earlier section is dropped to IDS. The reporting sections stay reporting-only. This is the discipline that makes the meeting possible.

## Why it matters

Three things compound when this meeting holds.

**The team gets a weekly truth check.** The scorecard, rocks, headlines, and to-dos surface every drift in the business within seven days. Nothing festers for a month before being addressed.

**Accountability becomes the default.** The To-Do List makes private commitments public. Gino documents a measured productivity jump from "a few out of 10 done" to "nine out of 10 done" after introducing weekly to-do review.

> "All human beings are procrastinators by nature. ... During the experimentation with running a client's weekly meetings, I discovered that the commitments that team members were making to each other from the previous week were not being carried out. ... Once everyone had to report on them the following week, the number of tasks completed went from a few out of 10 to nine out of 10. Productivity increased because people knew they were going to be held accountable."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

**Solving issues becomes the team's identity.** Sixty out of ninety minutes go to IDS. The team becomes good at naming and resolving the real problems because that is the largest block of its weekly time together.

## How to do it

Five non-negotiable rules. From Gino, verbatim:

> The meetings must
> 1. be on the same day each week,
> 2. be at the same time each week,
> 3. have the same printed agenda,
> 4. start on time, and
> 5. end on time.
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

Two roles to assign. **Meeting runner** moves the team through the agenda and keeps it on track. **Agenda manager** maintains the one-page agenda each week, updates the Scorecard and Rock Sheet, and keeps the To-Do and Issues Lists current.

One inviolable attendance rule. From Gino: *"The only reasons for missing the weekly Level 10 Meeting are vacation or death."* If a leadership-team member cannot attend, the meeting still runs.

One scoring habit. End every meeting with the round of 1-to-10 ratings. Average below 8 means you debug the meeting itself in next week's IDS.

## What good looks like

A leadership team that has run the Level 10 for six months looks like this.

The agenda fits on one sheet of paper. There are no meeting minutes, the agenda is the record. The Scorecard is in front of everyone with 5 to 15 numbers, most on-track. The Rock Sheet shows the quarter's priorities with named owners. The To-Do List has 90 percent of last week's items struck off. The Issues List has 5 to 15 issues, the team works the top three in priority order.

The meeting starts at the same time every week and ends 90 minutes later. The IDS section is passionate and intense. People disagree out loud, resolve the disagreement, and move on. Nobody is on their phone. Nobody walks out unsure what was decided.

The rating round at the end averages 8 or higher.

## Common mistakes

- **Discussing in the reporting sections.** The biggest failure mode. Someone reports an off-track number and the team immediately starts solving. The Scorecard, Rock review, headlines, and to-do sections are *reporting-only*. Off-track items get dropped to the IDS list and solved there, in priority order, with the rest of the issues.
- **Skipping or rescheduling.** A canceled Level 10 is a canceled heartbeat. The team rebuilds the cancel habit instead of the meeting habit. Hold it weekly even when the agenda looks light.
- **Letting it run long.** When the meeting runs over, the IDS section is what loses time next week, because the next meeting starts late and burns minutes. End on time is structural, not stylistic.
- **Treating the agenda as a starting point to be customized weekly.** It is the agenda. Once you have it dialed, you stop tinkering. Inventing new sections every week is a sign the team has not yet trusted the format.
- **No rating round.** Without weekly ratings the meeting silently degrades. The rating exposes degradation while it is still small.

## The reframe

Most leaders think their meeting problem is *how* the meeting is run. Their actual problem is that they do not have a meeting structure at all. The Level 10 is the structure: identical every week, ninety minutes, seven sections, sixty minutes on issues.

> "A weekly Level 10 Meeting keeps you focused on what's important, helps you spot developing problems, and then drives you to solve them. What makes for great meetings is solving problems. Patrick Lencioni says it best: 'Your meetings should be passionate, intense, exhausting, and never boring.'"
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007 (quoting Patrick Lencioni)

When the Level 10 is in place, almost every other operational complaint about the leadership team quietly resolves.
