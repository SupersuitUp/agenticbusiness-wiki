# The Weekly Scorecard

*Five to fifteen activity-based numbers, reviewed every week. Each number is owned by one person. The Scorecard predicts the P&L, the P&L confirms it after the fact. Without a Scorecard, you are flying blind between financial statements.*

*Distilled from [Gino Wickman](/mentors/gino-wickman), Traction: Get a Grip on Your Business, 2007. The Scorecard is the weekly truth-check at the heart of the EOS Data Component, reviewed in the second section of every [Level 10 Meeting](/principles/the-level-10-meeting).*

## What it is

The Scorecard is a one-page spreadsheet. Across the top: thirteen weekly columns (one quarter at a glance). Down the left: between five and fifteen rows, each one a single number the business runs on. Next to each row: the name of the person who owns that number, and the weekly goal.

Every week, the numbers get filled in. Every week, the leadership team reads the Scorecard in five minutes at the top of the Level 10 Meeting. Each row is reported as on-track or off-track. Off-track numbers drop straight to the IDS list and get solved later in the meeting.

That is the entire mechanic.

> "A Scorecard is a weekly report containing five to 15 high-level numbers for the organization. ... It will enable you to have a pulse of your business on a weekly basis, predict future developments, and quickly identify when things have fallen off the track."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

## Why it matters

Two old maxims hold here. Anything that gets measured and watched improves. And if the only data you see is the monthly P&L, you are responding to the past instead of shaping the future.

The Scorecard's specific job is to be *predictive*. The P&L tells you what already happened, the Scorecard tells you what is about to happen. Leads-to-contacts ratios this week predict closes two and three months out. Customer satisfaction ratings this week predict churn next quarter. Sales activity this week predicts revenue this quarter.

> "Your Scorecard predicts your P&L."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

When the Scorecard holds, the leadership team starts seeing problems six to twelve weeks before they would have hit the financial statements. That window is the difference between solving the problem and absorbing it.

## How to do it

**Identify the five to fifteen numbers that actually drive the business.** Closer to five is better. Brainstorm with the leadership team. The right numbers are *activity-based and leading*, not the lagging P&L outputs. Lead count, demos booked, proposals sent, customer satisfaction scores, gross margin per sale, accounts receivable aging, on-time delivery rate. These are the kinds of numbers that show up on a Scorecard. Monthly revenue total is not (it is a lagging output of the activity numbers above it).

> "Say, for instance, you choose number of new leads generated and track that number in the Scorecard. By knowing the number of leads you have, you can see how many of the leads turn into contacts, how many contacts turn into appointments, and so on. By understanding these formulas and these ratios, you'll be able to predict the number of closes two, three, and sometimes four months down the road."
>
> Gino Wickman, *Traction*, 2007

**Assign one owner per number.** Same discipline as [Rocks](/principles/rocks-before-sand): exactly one name per number. The owner is the person accountable for delivering that number, not the person who types it into the spreadsheet. Sales activity numbers are owned by the head of sales, not by the finance person who maintains the Scorecard.

**Set a weekly goal for each number.** The goal is what "on-track" means for that row. Without a goal, "on-track" becomes opinion.

**Designate one person to update the Scorecard every week.** This person collects the numbers from each owner and has the sheet ready before the Level 10 Meeting begins. They are not the owner of the numbers, they are the operator of the artifact.

**Show thirteen weeks at a glance.** Three months of weekly data lets the team see trends, not just snapshots. Once the fourteenth week is added, the first week rolls off the visible sheet (kept in archive for historical reference).

**Read it in five minutes at the top of every Level 10 Meeting.** Each row gets reported as on-track or off-track. No discussion in the Scorecard section. Off-track numbers drop to the IDS list and are solved there, in priority order.

## What good looks like

A leadership team running a Scorecard well has:

- A one-page Scorecard with 5 to 15 rows, ideally closer to 5
- Each row owned by exactly one person, with a written weekly goal
- Thirteen weeks of data visible at a glance
- The Scorecard updated every week and placed in front of every Level 10 attendee
- A five-minute Scorecard review at the top of the meeting, reporting only, no discussion
- Off-track numbers reliably dropped to the IDS list and worked in priority order
- Off-track items often red-flagged (shaded red) for visibility

When the discipline holds, the leadership team can answer "how is the business doing this week" with a finger pointed at five numbers, in less than thirty seconds.

## Common mistakes

- **Too many numbers.** A 30-row Scorecard is a 30-row tab on a spreadsheet that nobody reads. Stay closer to five than fifteen. The whole point is that the team can absorb it at a glance.
- **Tracking P&L outputs instead of activity inputs.** "Monthly revenue" is too late. The activity numbers that produce revenue (leads, demos, proposals) are the ones the Scorecard exists to surface.
- **No named owner per row.** When two people own a number, no one is accountable. When no one owns it, the number drifts and the row goes dead. One name per row.
- **Discussing in the Scorecard section.** The biggest Level 10 anti-pattern. Someone reports an off-track number and the team starts solving. Off-track items go straight to the IDS list. Discussion happens there, with all off-track items in front of the team, in priority order.
- **Not updating the Scorecard weekly.** A Scorecard that gets filled in once a month is a P&L with extra steps. The weekly cadence is the value.

## The reframe

Most teams think they need better dashboards. They do not, they need a smaller dashboard with the right numbers on it. Five activity-based numbers, owned by name, refreshed every week, reviewed at the top of every leadership meeting, is more valuable than a thirty-tab BI dashboard nobody opens.

The Scorecard is the cheapest, most boring instrument the operator has, and it is the one that tells them the truth a quarter before the P&L does.
