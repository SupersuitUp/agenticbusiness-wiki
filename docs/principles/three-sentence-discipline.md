## What it is

Most internal communication is structured for the convenience of the sender. Long emails, long Slack threads, long decks, with the actual ask buried somewhere in the middle and the supporting facts mixed with throat-clearing.

The three-sentence discipline reverses this. The sender does the compression work in advance. Every escalation, status update, or decision request fits into three sentences answering three questions in this order:

1. What is the problem?
2. What is the root cause?
3. What is the proposed solution?

Cost numbers, economics, and supporting detail can ride below the three sentences. The three sentences carry the load.

> "He's like, let's try to get down to three-sentence emails. What is the problem? What's your analysis of the root cause? And what's your proposed solution? And he's like, I think we could run the company on three-sentence emails. And so we did that."
>
> — Jon McNeill, on Elon Musk's communication standard at Tesla, 2026-04-09

The same pattern is articulated differently by other operators. Emmett Shear at Twitch teaches three sentences as "What. Why. So what." The underlying discipline is identical.

## Why it works

The CEO's most precious commodity is information processing time. Every minute a senior decision-maker spends extracting an ask from a long message is a minute the organization is waiting on a decision. The three-sentence format compresses absorption time, processing time, and decision time at once.

The receiver wins, but so does the sender. Forcing the message into three sentences is the most reliable way to discover that the sender did not actually have clarity on the problem in the first place. The sender thought they had clarity. The compression reveals they were one analytical step short. The discipline produces sharper thinking because the format will not accept fuzziness.

The compression also makes the work auditable. Every three-sentence escalation is a small record of how the sender was framing the problem at that moment. When the decision later proves right or wrong, the record explains which step was off: the framing of the problem, the analysis of the root cause, or the proposed solution.

## How to use it

**Adopt the format yourself first.** Send your own escalations in three sentences. Your team will mimic what they see from you faster than they will respond to a memo announcing the format.

**Refuse longer escalations.** When a team member sends a long message, ask them to collapse it. Do it once. They will collapse the next one on their own.

**Allow detail below the three sentences, not above them.** Numbers, supporting context, and follow-up data ride below the three sentences in a clearly separated section. The three sentences come first because the reader makes the decision off them.

**Apply it upward too.** Communicating to an acquiring CEO, a board chair, or any senior decision-maker, the same discipline saves their time and earns trust faster than any other communication habit.

## Worked example

Elon Musk and McNeill ran much of Tesla through three-sentence emails. A typical escalation:

- *Problem: Model X door-fit first-time-pass rate is at 41 percent; target is 95 percent.*
- *Root cause: technicians are threading the Falcon Wing bolt at an angle they cannot see, leading to misalignment.*
- *Proposed solution: maintenance team builds a jig that holds the bolt at the correct angle; pilot on Line 3 this week.*

The reader makes a decision in seconds. If more detail is needed, it sits below. If a decision can be made on the three sentences alone, the loop closes immediately.

McNeill describes adopting the same pattern at every company he has run since Tesla, and teaching his teams to send him three sentences and to expect three sentences back.

## What good looks like

An operator using this discipline:

- Sends their own escalations in three sentences with detail below
- Has a team that mimics the format upward without being asked
- Can run a leadership meeting on three-sentence pre-reads instead of decks
- Treats long unstructured messages as a signal that the sender has not yet finished thinking
- Notices when an attempt to compress fails, and uses that failure as the diagnostic that more analytical work is needed before the message can be sent

## Common mistakes

- **Faking the format.** Three sentences padded with adjectives is still a long message. The discipline is real compression, not surface compliance.
- **Skipping the root cause.** Sender goes problem-to-solution and leaves the diagnosis implicit. The receiver cannot evaluate the proposed solution without seeing the analysis.
- **Refusing to use it on hard problems.** "It's too complicated for three sentences" is almost always a sign that the sender has not yet done the compression work. Hard problems are exactly the ones that benefit most.
- **Using it as a hazing tool.** The format exists to save the organization time, not to make the sender feel small. Coach the team into it, do not punish them with it.
